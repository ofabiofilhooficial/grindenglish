Based on Huberman’s Monthly Newsletter (Apr 2024)

Here I explain how to set and achieve your goals. These topics have been discussed in several Huberman Lab episodes including The Science of Setting & Achieving Goals and Goals Toolkit: How to Set & Achieve Your Goals.
Goal Setting: 6 Steps to Make the Process Easier and Make You More Productive
To begin, you’ll need to pick a goal. That might seem obvious. But many people fail to achieve their goals. Why? They don’t spend time defining their specific goal(s). To do so, follow steps #1-6:
Step 1: Choose a Priority Goal
During goal setting, people often want to overhaul too many habits at once. For example, start a diet, workout, sleep more and start meditating. All starting January 1st. Pursuing too many goals simultaneously often leads to failure of all goals. Instead, set a priority goal by asking: What is the one goal I can give the necessary time and energy to pursue right now?
Step 2: Pursue a Lofty Goal
Your chosen goal should be ambitious. Simple goals won’t cause sufficient levels of neural arousal and stress to stimulate real growth and learning. Therefore, aim for a lofty goal.
Step 3: Define Specific Actions
While overarching goals like “get fit,” “write a book” or “run more” are helpful to list during brainstorming, you must identify the actions involved in achieving each of these ‘macro’-goals. Break down your desired outcome into specific actions steps to significantly improve your likelihood of achieving your goal. For example, run three times per week.
Step 4: Goal Measurability
Establish a finite time course to complete your goal; I’d suggest a 12-week time period is appropriate for most goals. Within this quarterly timeframe, define:
Total hours per week
Specific days
The length of each goal-oriented work session
A specific time window (e.g., from 8 a.m. to 9 a.m.) or a time constraint (e.g., before 9 a.m.) for the goal-oriented work session
Step 5: Quantify Your Goals
Some goals have easily quantifiable outcomes (e.g., running a marathon in less than a given amount of time). Others are more subjective (e.g., developing a meditation practice). With loosely defined goal outcomes, take extra care. Outline the specific, necessary actions for your plan.
For any type of goal, it can also help to designate smaller outcomes along the 12-week journey. They will keep you feeling motivated.
Step 6: Don’t Tell the World!
Have you ever started a goal and wanted to share this news via group text or a social media post? Unfortunately, receiving positive feedback on a newly set goal can undermine your success. This positive encouragement can activate the brain’s reward mechanisms. This reduces your motivation to continue pursuing the goal. You already got the reward without the hard work. So, when you start a goal, it’s usually best to keep this endeavor to yourself. It can also make sharing with friends and family more fun once you’ve hit your first milestone.
Goal Pursuit: 7 Tools to Make It Easier and More Effective
Once you have specified your goal and outlined the necessary action steps, use tools #1-7. They will help you to stay motivated and reach your goal.
Tool 1: The Post-it Note Fallacy
Placing a note on the mirror that exclaims “floss!” or “run today!” might be motivating at first, but likely, after a few days, you fail to “see” this visual reminder anymore. The brain monitors for anything new in our environment. But it doesn’t call our attention to the day-to-day similarities. A static visual reminder will fail to trigger action. Instead, write a new note every few days and post it in a different location.
Tool 2: Visualize the End and Visualize What Failure Looks Like
Yes failure. If you feel motivated before the work session, spend one to three minutes visualizing the positive outcomes of goal success as well as goal failure. Do this to sustain motivation through your work bout. On unmotivated days, spend one to three minutes visualizing the failure of not achieving your goal. That is, visualize the negative outcome. Humans will work very hard to avoid feelings of failure. This visualization exercise focuses on feelings of failure. It will recruit neurochemicals, like adrenaline, to boost your levels of arousal and focus.
Tool 3: Visual Targets
Maintaining mental focus is strongly anchored to the visual system. Use a visual target strategy. Narrow the “cone” of your visual attention to a specific object or point in front of you. Hold it in your gaze for 30-90 seconds. Dr. Emily Balcetis and colleagues have shown that this exercise increases alertness and autonomic arousal in the brain and body. It also helps to build and sustain motivation.
Tip: Relax your gaze and look off to the horizon when you finish working. This “turns off” the release of chemicals associated with alertness and arousal and will aid in relaxation.
Tool 4: Put Your Phone Away!
During your work bouts, put your phone out of reach or turn on airplane mode. Both task-switching and procrastinating can eat up your work time. Task-switching is when you look at your phone between bouts of work. Procrastinating is when you look at your phone right before starting the work.
Tool 5: Intermittent Rewards
Motivation and the associated neurochemical dopamine have been the topic of many Huberman Lab episodes. In brief, consistently receiving rewards diminishes the reward’s power and reduces overall motivation. In contrast, only rewarding yourself at the finish line (~12 weeks) also reduces motivation.
The solution? Use random, intermittent rewards (like a slot machine) to maintain motivation. After your goal work, flip a coin: heads = reward vs. tails = no reward.
On a reward day, try using positive self-talk. Do it for 30-60 seconds to reinforce the neural circuits associated with internal motivation. Treat yourself to a physical reward from time to time. For example, watch a movie or eat a food you enjoy!
Tool 6: The Middle Problem
People tend to be highly motivated to start and finish a goal. But motivation often declines in the middle. To overcome the middle problem:
Acknowledge this is a normal phenomenon
Use time chunking to break-up the middle portion into 3-4 smaller time segments
Tool 7: Circadian Rhythms & Attention
There are specific times during the day that are for optimal goal work. Dr. Pablo Valdez describes three peak times each day when our attention and focus are at their highest. As a rough guide, 30 minutes, 3 hours, and 11 hours after you wake up are great times for hard work. This happens because of the body’s circadian rhythm (sleep-wake cycle) and rhythmicity of body temperature and associated neurochemicals.

Punctuation: Always start with a Capital letter. Always end with a Full stop (.) or Question mark (?).

3) Meaning
English is a "Fixed Word Order" language. The position of the word tells us who is doing the action.
4) Use
Use this structure for 90% of your basic communication. If you move the words (e.g., Coffee drink I), people might get confused.
5) Contrast
Correct: I like apples.
Incorrect: Like I apples. (Common in languages where word order is flexible).
6) Classic mistakes
Missing Subject: "Is a teacher" → Fix: "He is a teacher."
Lowercase "I": "am happy" → Fix: "I am happy." (The word "I" is always a capital).

4) Use (when to choose it)
Can you...? is the standard, everyday way to ask family, friends, and coworkers for help.
Can I have...? is the most common way to order food or buy things in casual settings.
Pragmatic note: Can is casual but polite enough for most daily interactions, provided you add "please" at the end of the question.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Can I have... vs. I want...
"I want a coffee" sounds demanding and often rude to native English speakers. Always use "Can I have..." or "I'd like..." when interacting with service staff.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Dropping the subject pronoun (Can help me? / Can open the window?).
Fix: English questions must always have a subject. You must specify who is doing the action (Can you help me?).
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, it is very common to ask favors using just the verb (Pode me ajudar?). Remind learners that the "you" is mandatory in English.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Can you send the email? → Upgrade: Could you send the email, please? (A bit softer and more polite).
Starter: Can I have the menu? → Upgrade: Could I get the menu, please?
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Find and correct the mistake.
Can you to close the door, please?
I want a sandwich, please.
Can pass me the water?
Guided: What would you say in these situations?
You are at a cafe and want a black coffee: ____________________.

A1.G.08. Present continuous
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Describe what is happening at this exact moment.
Talk about temporary situations.
Describe actions in a photograph or video.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + be (am/is/are) + verb-ing
Affirmative (+)
Negative (-)
Question (?)
I am ('m) working.
I am not ('m not) working.
Am I working?
She is ('s) studying.
She is not (isn't) studying.
Is she studying?
They are ('re) eating.
They are not (aren't) eating.
Are they eating?

Spelling rules for adding -ing:
Most verbs: just add -ing (play → playing).
Verbs ending in -e: drop the -e, add -ing (make → making).
One-syllable verbs ending in Consonant-Vowel-Consonant (CVC): double the final consonant (sit → sitting, run → running).
3) Meaning (what it means)
The action is in progress right now. It started in the past, it is happening at this second, and it will finish in the future. It is temporary.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Choose this tense when answering the question: "What are you doing?" or "What's happening?"
Common time markers: now, right now, at the moment, currently.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Present Simple (Habit/Fact): I work in an office. (General truth).
Present Continuous (Now/Temporary): I am working from home today. (Temporary situation).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Forgetting the verb be (I working right now / She studying).
Fix: The -ing cannot stand alone. It always needs am/is/are to anchor it to the present.
Error: Using simple present for actions happening right now (Look! It rains).
Fix: If you can point at it happening, use the continuous (Look! It is raining).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I am studying English now. → Upgrade: I'm currently focusing on my English.
Starter: What do you do now? (Incorrect) → Upgrade: What are you working on at the moment?

Guided: Write true sentences about right now.

Error: Adding prepositions unnecessarily (I like of music).
Fix: English does not use "of" after like, love, or hate.

A1.G.11. Past simple (regular verbs)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Tell chronological stories about your life.
Report what you did at work yesterday or over the weekend.
Give a sequence of finished events.
2) Form (how to build it)
Affirmative (+): Subject + Verb-ed
I worked late yesterday.
She lived in Paris in 2015.
Spelling Rules for -ed:
Most verbs: add -ed (start → started, watch → watched).
Verbs ending in -e: add -d (arrive → arrived, decide → decided).
Verbs ending in consonant + y: change 'y' to 'i' and add -ed (study → studied, try → tried).
One-syllable verbs ending in CVC: double the final consonant (stop → stopped, plan → planned).
3) Meaning (what it means)
The past simple describes an action that started and finished in the past. It is over. It has no connection to right now.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Choose this tense when you can answer the question "When did it happen?" (Even if you don't say the time out loud, the time is finished in your mind).
Common triggers: ago (two days ago), last (last month), in + past year (in 1999).
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
I live in Brazil. (Present state - Fact now)
I lived in London for two years. (Past state - I don't live there now).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Pronouncing the "-ed" as an extra syllable on every verb (e.g., saying "work-ed" or "play-ed").
Fix: The "-ed" only adds an extra syllable /ɪd/ if the original verb ends in a T or a D sound (want → want-ed, need → need-ed). For all other verbs, it just sounds like a quick /t/ or /d/ at the end (worked = "workt", played = "playd").
L1 Transfer Note: Because Portuguese reads every vowel, Brazilians naturally want to pronounce the 'e' in '-ed'. It takes conscious effort to silence that 'e'. Drill the difference between "wanted" (extra syllable) and "stopped" (no extra syllable).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: First I worked, then I cooked. → Upgrade: After I finished work, I cooked dinner.
Starter: I studied a lot yesterday. → Upgrade: I spent the whole day studying.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Write the past tense form and say it out loud. Does it have an extra syllable?
wait → ___________ (Extra syllable? Yes/No)
call → ___________ (Extra syllable? Yes/No)
finish → ___________ (Extra syllable? Yes/No)
Guided: Complete the sentences about yesterday.
Yesterday morning, I _______________.
Last night, I _______________.

A1.G.12. Past simple questions/negatives
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Ask people about their past experiences and weekends.
Deny that you did something.
Clarify details in a conversation.
2) Form (how to build it)
Negative (-): Subject + didn't + Base Verb
I didn't go to work yesterday.
She didn't like the movie.
Questions (?): Did + Subject + Base Verb
Did you watch the game?
Where did they eat?
Starter Irregular Verbs (Base → Past)
go → went
have → had
do → did
eat → ate
buy → bought
3) Meaning (what it means)
These structures allow you to investigate or correct facts about the finished past.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use did and didn't for all subjects (I, you, he, she, it, we, they). It never changes.
Crucial Rule: "Did" acts like a time machine. It carries the past tense so the main verb doesn't have to. The main verb stays in the base form.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Affirmative: He went home. (Main verb changes to past).
Negative: He didn't go home. (Auxiliary 'didn't' shows past; main verb is base).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: The "Double Past" - changing the main verb in questions and negatives (Did you went? / I didn't saw him).
Fix: Remember the time machine rule. If "did" or "didn't" is in the sentence, the main verb relaxes in its base form. (Did you go? / I didn't see him).
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the main verb changes in questions and negatives (Você foi? / Eu não vi). English splits the job: the auxiliary does the grammar (time/negative), and the main verb does the meaning.
Error: Using wasn't/weren't with action verbs (I wasn't eat breakfast).
Fix: Only use was/were for states and locations. For actions, use didn't.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Did you have a good weekend? → Upgrade: How was your weekend? / Get up to anything fun over the weekend?
Starter: I didn't do anything. → Upgrade: I just relaxed at home. / Nothing special.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Find and correct the mistake.
Did she finished the report?
I didn't bought the tickets yet.
Where you went last night?
Guided: Turn these affirmative sentences into negatives.
We watched TV all night. → We ____________________ TV all night.
He had a meeting at 9:00. → He ____________________ a meeting at 9:00.

Error: Forgetting the verb be (I going to buy a car).

A1.G.14. Future: will
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Make quick, spontaneous decisions in the moment.
Order food at a restaurant or drinks at a bar.
Offer to help someone or make a promise.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + will / won't + base verb
Affirmative (+)
Negative (-)
Question (?)
I will ('ll) help you.
I will not (won't) tell anyone.
Will you help me?
She will ('ll) call later.
She won't call later.
Will she call later?

3) Meaning (what it means)
Unlike be going to, will is used when the idea just popped into your head. There is no prior plan. It is a reaction to the present moment.
4) Use (when to choose it)

Will vs. Be going to (The ultimate test)
A: "We don't have any milk."
B (Spontaneous decision): "Really? I will ('ll) go to the store and buy some."

Error: Using will for established plans (Next year, I will visit Disney).

A1.G.15. Comparatives
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Compare two products before buying one.
Talk about the differences between two cities or two people.
Justify your preferences (e.g., why A is better than B).
2) Form (how to build it)
Rule 1: Short Adjectives (1 syllable)
Add -er + than:
tall → taller than
cheap → cheaper than.
Spelling: If it ends in CVC, double the last letter (big → bigger). If it ends in 'y', change to 'i' (busy → busier).
Rule 2: Long Adjectives (2+ syllables)
Add more + adjective + than: expensive → more expensive than, comfortable → more comfortable than.
Rule 3: Irregular Adjectives (Memorize these)
good → better than
bad → worse than
far → further/farther than
3) Meaning (what it means)
You are placing two nouns on a scale and showing the difference between them.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Always use this structure when contrasting exactly two things.
If the second thing is obvious from context, you can drop "than + noun" (e.g., "This laptop is $500, but that one is $400. That one is cheaper.")
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Comparing two things vs. describing one thing.
Fact: My car is fast.
Comparison: My car is faster than your car.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Mixing both rules (He is more taller than me).
Fix: Choose one. Short words get -er. Long words get more. Never both.
Error: Using that instead of than (Brazil is bigger that France).
Fix: That points to things. Than connects comparisons.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the word que translates to both that and than, making this a very common mistake. Drill than as the official "comparison bridge." Portuguese also uses mais for all comparisons (mais alto, mais caro), so students often forget the -er suffix entirely and say more tall.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: New York is more expensive than Blumenau. → Upgrade: New York is much more expensive than Blumenau. / New York is a lot more expensive.
Starter: John is taller than Mark. → Upgrade: John is a little bit taller than Mark.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Write the comparative form.
safe → _______________
interesting → _______________
bad → _______________
Guided: Compare the two items using the adjective in parentheses.

English / Mathematics (difficult) → ________________________________________.

A1.G.16. Superlatives
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Talk about extremes (the number one spot in a category).
Recommend the absolute best places or experiences.
Describe the limits of your experience (e.g., the worst movie you ever saw).
2) Form (how to build it)
Rule 1: Short Adjectives (1 syllable)
Use the + adjective + -est: 
tall → the tallest
cheap → the cheapest
Rule 2: Long Adjectives (2+ syllables)
Use the most + adjective: expensive → the most expensive.
Rule 3: Irregular Adjectives
good → the best
bad → the worst
far → the furthest/farthest
3) Meaning (what it means)
While comparatives put two things on a scale, superlatives put one thing at the very top (or very bottom) of a group of three or more.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use superlatives with place modifiers like in the world, in my city, in the company.
Do NOT use than with superlatives.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Comparative (1 vs 1): The book is better than the movie.
Superlative (1 vs All): This is the best book in the library.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Forgetting "the" (He is tallest player on the team).
Fix: The superlative always demands "the" because there is only one number-one spot. It is specific.
Error: Using "of" for places (The tallest building of the city).
Fix: English prefers "in" for geographic areas and places (The tallest building in the city / in the world).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: It is the best restaurant. → Upgrade: It's one of the best restaurants in town. (Softer, more realistic).
Starter: This is the most expensive car. → Upgrade: This is by far the most expensive car here. (Adds emphasis).
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Write the superlative form.

A1.G.17. Basic modals: must / have to / should
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Give advice to friends and colleagues.
Explain the rules at your workplace or in your country.
Talk about obligations and things that are necessary.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + Modal + Base Verb
You should talk to the boss. (Advice)
I have to wear a uniform. (External Rule)
I must finish this today. (Internal Obligation / Strong Rule)
Negative Forms:
You shouldn't smoke. (Bad idea)
You don't have to pay. (Not necessary - it is a choice)
You mustn't touch that. (Strict prohibition)
Rules: No 's' for he/she/it (except has to). No to after the modal (except the built-in to in have to).
3) Meaning (what it means)
Should: 50% power. It's a good idea, but you have a choice.
Have to: 100% power. A rule from the outside (boss, law, society). You have no choice.
Must: 100% power. A rule from the inside (your own conscience) or a very formal written rule.
4) Use (when to choose it)
In spoken English, have to is much more common than must for everyday rules.
Use should when someone asks you, "What do you think I should do?"
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Mustn't vs. Don't have to (Crucial Difference)
You mustn't drive on the left side of the road in Brazil. (It is illegal; do not do it).
You don't have to wear a suit to the party. (It is optional; you can if you want, but it's not required).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Adding to after must or should (You should to go now).
Fix: Modals are allergic to the word to. (You should go now).
Error: Confusing must and have to in negatives.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, não precisa covers both optional things and sometimes prohibitions. Make sure learners understand that don't have to means "lack of obligation," not "prohibition."
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: You should see a doctor. → Upgrade: If I were you, I'd see a doctor. / It might be a good idea to see a doctor. (Softer advice).
Starter: I have to go. → Upgrade: I've got to go. / I gotta go. (Highly natural spoken English).
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose the correct modal.

A1.G.18. Prepositions of time
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Schedule meetings with precision.
Talk about your birthday, national holidays, and routines.
Give deadlines for tasks.
2) Form (how to build it)
IN (The biggest time periods)
Centuries/Years/Months: in 2025, in July.
Seasons: in the summer, in the winter.
Parts of the day: in the morning, in the afternoon, in the evening.
ON (Days and Dates - Medium periods)
Days of the week: on Monday, on Fridays.
Specific dates: on July 4th, on my birthday.
AT (Specific points in time - Small periods)
Clock times: at 3:00 PM, at 7:30.
Specific moments/holidays: at noon, at midnight, at Christmas (the holiday period).
3) Meaning (what it means)
Prepositions "zoom in" on time. Think of an upside-down triangle. In is the wide top (general time). On is the middle (specific days). At is the tiny point at the bottom (exact times).
4) Use (when to choose it)
When combining times, we usually start with the time (at), then the day (on).
Example: "The meeting is at 10:00 AM on Tuesday."
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
in the morning / in the afternoon / in the evening.
BUT: at night. (This is a historical exception; memorize it!).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Translating directly from the native language (In Monday / At July).
Fix: Visualize the triangle. Monday is a day → ON. July is a month → IN.
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese uses em (no/na) for almost everything (na segunda, em julho, às 3). This requires pure memorization of the English triangle system.
Error: Using a preposition with words like next, last, this, every (I'll see you on next Monday).
Fix: When using next, last, this, or every, drop the preposition completely. (I'll see you next Monday).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: The meeting is on Friday at 3:00. → Upgrade: Does Friday at 3:00 work for you?
Starter: I will do it in the morning. → Upgrade: I'll get it done first thing in the morning.

Controlled: Fill in the blanks with in, on, or at.

Guided: Answer the questions.

How + Adjective/Quantifier + Auxiliary + Subject + Verb?

A1.G.21. Basic conjunctions II
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Explain your reasons for doing something.
Describe the results of an action.
Connect simple, short sentences into longer, more fluent ideas.
2) Form (how to build it)
Reason: Because
[Situation/Result] + because + [Reason]
I went to bed early because I was tired.
We stayed home because it was raining.
Result: So
[Reason] + , so + [Result]
I was tired**, so** I went to bed early.
It was raining**, so** we stayed home.
3) Meaning (what it means) These two words are two sides of the same coin. Because points backward to the cause. So points forward to the effect.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use because to answer "Why?" questions.
Use so to narrate a story chronologically (Action A happened, so Action B happened).
Punctuation note: Usually, we put a comma before so. We do not put a comma before because.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Because vs. Because of (Intro)
I am late because there was traffic. (Because + subject + verb)
I am late because of the traffic. (Because of + noun).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Starting a sentence with "Because" and leaving it incomplete (Why are you late? Because the bus was late.)
Fix: In spoken English, this is perfectly fine! However, in formal writing, because needs to connect two ideas in the same sentence. (I am late because the bus was late).
Error: Confusing so and to for purpose (I went to the store so buy milk).
Fix: Use to for the purpose of an action (I went to the store to buy milk). Use so for a logical result (I had no milk, so I went to the store).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I was sick, so I didn't go. → Upgrade: I was sick. That's why I didn't go.
Starter: I like it because it's cheap. → Upgrade: I like it mostly because it's cheap.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose because or so.
I didn't study, ___________ I failed the test.
She is learning Spanish ___________ she wants to move to Madrid.
The restaurant was full, ___________ we went to a different one.
Guided: Finish the sentences logically.
I am studying English because ____________________.
I wanted a new phone, so ____________________.
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: Tell a partner a short story about a time things went wrong on a trip or at work. Use because twice and so twice.

A1.G.22. Basic object + infinitive
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)

Subject + Verb + to + Base Verb

Negatives:
I don't want to go.

A1.G.23. Basic “too/enough”
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Evaluate products or situations.
Complain about problems (temperature, size, price).
Explain why you cannot do something.
2) Form (how to build it)
Too (More than you need/want)
too + adjective
The shoes are too small.
It is too expensive.
Enough (Exactly what you need/want)
adjective + enough
The shoes are big enough.
Is the water warm enough?
Not enough (Less than you need/want)
not + adjective + enough
It is not big enough.
3) Meaning (what it means) These words judge limits. Too means a limit has been crossed, and it is usually a negative thing. Enough means the requirement has been successfully met.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Very common in shopping scenarios (clothes, houses, cars) and talking about weather or food.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Very vs. Too
Very just makes an adjective stronger. It can be good or bad. (This car is very fast = I like it! / It is very expensive = Just a fact).
Too means there is a problem. (This car is too fast = I am scared. / It is too expensive = I cannot buy it).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting enough before the adjective (This shirt is enough big).
Fix: Enough always comes after the adjective. (This shirt is big enough).
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the word order is flexible or reversed (É grande o suficiente or É suficientemente grande). In English, the order is locked: Adjective first, enough second.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: It is too expensive. → Upgrade: It's a bit out of my price range.
Starter: It is not big enough. → Upgrade: I think I need a larger size.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Put the words in the correct order.

Define words you don't know in English (circumlocution).

2) Form (how to build it)

Noun (Person) + who + Verb...

I like people who are honest.
For Things: use THAT
Noun (Thing) + that + Verb...
It is a machine that makes coffee.

3) Meaning (what it means) Relative clauses act like one giant adjective. They attach directly to a noun to give essential information about it, answering the question: "Which one?"
4) Use (when to choose it)

Two sentences vs. One clause
Basic: I have a friend. He speaks Japanese.
Better: I have a friend who speaks Japanese.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)

Error: Using who for things, or which for people.
Fix: Keep it strict at A1: Who is for humans. That is for objects/animals.

Starter: A person who makes food is a chef. → Upgrade: Basically, it's a person who makes food.

have → had
buy → bought
see → saw

A2.G.03. Present perfect
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Network and find common ground with new people.
Answer interview questions about your skills and history.
Brag about exciting things you have accomplished.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + have/has + Past Participle (V3)
I have ('ve) been to Japan.
She has ('s) eaten sushi.
We haven't seen that movie.
Using Ever and Never:
Questions: Have you ever worked in sales? (At any point in your life).
Negative: I have never worked in sales. (Zero times in my life).
3) Meaning (what it means)
When you use the present perfect for experience, you are talking about your life as an unfinished timeline. You are not saying when something happened, just that the experience currently exists in your brain.
4) Use (when to choose it)
The "Have you ever...?" loop: This is the most common conversation starter in English.
Person A: "Have you ever been to Paris?" (Present Perfect - establishing the experience).
Person B: "Yes, I have. I went there in 2019." (Past Simple - shifting to the specific details).
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Past Simple: I went to London in 2015. (Focus is on the specific date).
Present Perfect: I have been to London. (Focus is on the fact that I possess this experience).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using past simple to ask about life experiences (Did you ever eat snails?).
Fix: Always use Have you ever...? to ask about someone's life timeline.
L1 Transfer Note: This is a major hurdle. In Portuguese, asking about life experience uses the simple past with já (Você já comeu escargot?). Using the direct translation (Did you already eat?) sounds unnatural in English. Drill "Have you ever..." as a single, unbreakable chunk of vocabulary.
Error: Using gone instead of been (I have gone to Italy three times).
Fix: Been means you went and came back (experience). Gone means you went and are still there!
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Have you ever eaten Thai food? → Upgrade: Have you tried the new Thai place downtown?
Starter: I have never done that. → Upgrade: I've never done that before, but I'd love to try.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose the correct option.
(Did you ever go / Have you ever been) to a music festival?
I (have seen / saw) that movie last night.
She knows a lot about software because she (has worked / worked) in IT for years.
Guided: Write true sentences about your experiences.
I have never ____________________, but I want to.
I have ____________________ a few times in my life.

A2.G.04. Present perfect II
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Give immediate updates on a project's status.
Offer a reason why you don't want to do something (e.g., "I've already eaten").
Ask if a deadline has been met.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + have/has + [just / already / yet] + V3
Just (A few moments ago): I have just finished the report. (Goes in the middle).
Already (Sooner than expected): I have already paid the bill. (Goes in the middle).
Yet (Expected, but hasn't happened): * Questions: Have you sent the email yet? (Goes at the end).
Negatives: I haven't sent it yet. (Goes at the end).
3) Meaning (what it means)
While A2.G.03 looked at your whole life, this structure looks at the very recent past. The action just finished, and the result is highly visible or important right now.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use already to refuse offers politely: "Would you like some coffee?" -> "No thanks, I've already had two cups."
Use yet to manage expectations: "Is the meeting ready?" -> "Not yet, I haven't set up the projector yet."
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Past Simple: I finished my homework at 4:00. (Cold, historical fact).
Present Perfect: Can I watch TV? I've just finished my homework! (Hot news! The result matters right now).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting yet in the middle of the sentence (I haven't yet finished).
Fix: Yet is heavy; it sinks to the very end of the sentence.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, já acts as a wildcard for already, ever, now, and sometimes yet. (Você já terminou?). Brazilians often translate this to: "Did you already finish?" Instruct learners that in English questions expecting a result, yet at the end of the sentence is the most natural choice: "Have you finished yet?"

A2.G.05. Future forms review
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Manage your calendar and schedule appointments.
Make spontaneous offers to help others.
Clearly explain your intentions versus your fixed plans.
2) Form (how to build it)
Will (Decisions/Offers): I'll call you back.
Be going to (Intentions/Plans): I'm going to study tonight.
Present Continuous (Fixed Arrangements): I'm meeting the boss at 3 PM tomorrow.
3) Meaning (what it means)
Will: The decision was made at the exact moment of speaking. It popped into your head.
Going to: The decision was made in the past. It is an intention, but it might only involve you.
Present Continuous: The decision is locked in. It usually involves other people, a specific time, and an entry in your calendar.
4) Use (when to choose it)
If a colleague asks, "Can we meet tomorrow?", do NOT use will. Look at your calendar and say: "Sorry, I'm having lunch with a client at 12, but I'm going to be free after 2 PM."
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Going to vs. Present Continuous
I'm going to travel to Europe someday. (Intention. No tickets bought).
I'm traveling to Europe next Friday. (Arrangement. Tickets bought, bags packed).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using Present Simple for future arrangements (Tomorrow I travel to São Paulo).
Fix: Unless it is a bus or train schedule, do not use Present Simple for the future. Use the Present Continuous. (Tomorrow I am traveling to São Paulo).
L1 Transfer Note: Because Portuguese leans heavily on the simple present (Amanhã eu viajo) or vou + infinitive (Vou viajar) for the future, the English Present Continuous feels completely wrong to Brazilian learners because it looks like it means "right now." Reassure them that native speakers use this constantly for future diary events.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I will help you with that. → Upgrade: Let me take care of that for you.
Starter: I am going to the doctor tomorrow at 4. → Upgrade: I have a doctor's appointment tomorrow at 4.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose the best future form.
Phone rings. "I (will / am going to) answer it!"
I can't meet you on Friday. I (will fly / am flying) to Miami for a conference.

Guided: Finish the sentences truly.
After this lesson, I am going to ____________________.
Next weekend, I am ____________________ (use present continuous for a fixed plan).

A2.G.06. Modals for obligation/permission II
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Read and explain workplace guidelines or city laws.
Grant or deny permission professionally.
Clarify what tasks are optional.
2) Form (how to build it)
Obligation: You must wear a badge. / You have to wear a badge.
No Obligation (Optional): You don't have to work on Saturday.
Prohibition (Forbidden): You mustn't park here. / You can't park here.
Permission: You can leave early. / You are allowed to leave early.
3) Meaning (what it means)
This grammar dictates the rules of society. It tells you what is necessary, what is a choice, and what will get you into trouble.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Can is the most common way to give permission in everyday speech.
Be allowed to is used to talk about the rules from a distance (e.g., "We are allowed to wear jeans on Fridays").
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Mustn't vs. Don't have to (Review from A1)
You mustn't tell the boss. (It is a secret. Keep your mouth shut).
You don't have to tell the boss. (It's up to you. I don't care if you do or don't).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using can when you mean must (You can pay your taxes).
Fix: Can means you have a choice. Paying taxes is not a choice! Use have to.
L1 Transfer Note: Brazilians often confuse have to and must in the negative forms, as mentioned in A1. At the A2 level, reinforce that não precisa = don't have to, and não pode = can't / mustn't. Another common error is adding to after mustn't (You mustn't to go).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: You mustn't do that. → Upgrade: That's strictly prohibited. / You're not supposed to do that.
Starter: You don't have to come. → Upgrade: It's completely optional. / No pressure to come.

Controlled: Choose the correct modal phrase.

A2.G.07. First conditional
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Make plans that depend on the weather or other people.
Give warnings or make promises in the workplace.
Negotiate basic terms (e.g., "If you lower the price, I will buy it").
2) Form (how to build it)
The "If" Clause (Condition) + The Main Clause (Result)
If + Subject + Present Simple, Subject + will + Base Verb
If it rains, we will stay home.
If you help me, I will buy you lunch.
Reversing the order: You can put the result first. If you do, drop the comma.
We will stay home if it rains.
3) Meaning (what it means)
The first conditional talks about a real, possible situation in the future. It is not a fantasy; it is a highly likely cause and effect.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use this for practical, everyday planning and problem-solving.
It is the standard grammar for making a deal.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Two separate sentences: It might rain. We will stay home. (Disconnected).
First conditional: If it rains, we will stay home. (Logically linked).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting will in the If clause (If it will rain, we will stay home).
Fix: If acts as a barrier. The word will cannot go inside the If clause. It must stay in the result half of the sentence.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the future subjunctive is used here (Se chover / Se eu for). Because learners want to express a future idea, they often reach for will. Drill the formula: If + Present.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: If you call, I will answer. → Upgrade: Give me a call if you need anything.
Starter: If we don't hurry, we will be late. → Upgrade: We**'ll miss the flight if** we don't leave right now.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Find and correct the mistake.

Guided: Finish the sentence logically.

A2.G.08. Zero conditional
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Explain scientific facts and absolute truths.
Give instructions on how machines or software work.
Describe your non-negotiable personal routines.
2) Form (how to build it)
If/When + Present Simple, Present Simple
If you heat ice, it melts.
When I have a headache, I take medicine.
The machine turns off if you press this button.
3) Meaning (what it means) The zero conditional is not about the future; it is about always. It describes a 100% guaranteed result. If A happens, B always happens.
4) Use (when to choose it)
You can almost always replace If with When or Whenever in the zero conditional without changing the meaning.
Highly useful in IT, technical support, and HR (explaining company rules).
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
First Conditional (Future/Specific) vs. Zero Conditional (Always/General)
First (Specific day): If it rains tomorrow, I will take the bus.
Zero (General habit): If it rains, I take the bus. (Every time it rains, this is my routine).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using will for general facts (If you mix blue and yellow, you will get green).
Fix: While native speakers sometimes do this, it's better to stick to the present simple for absolute facts to sound more authoritative. (If you mix blue and yellow, you get green).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: If I drink coffee at night, I don't sleep. → Upgrade: Whenever I drink coffee late, I can't fall asleep.
Starter: If the screen is black, push the button. → Upgrade: When in doubt, just restart the computer.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose First Conditional or Zero Conditional based on the context.

Guided: Explain a rule or fact.

A2.G.09. Gerunds vs infinitives
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Chain multiple actions together smoothly.
Talk about your likes, dislikes, plans, and avoidance strategies.
State your future intentions clearly.
2) Form (how to build it)
When you put two verbs next to each other, the first verb acts as the boss. It decides what uniform the second verb must wear: the to-infinitive (to do) or the gerund (-ing).
Group A: The "to-infinitive" Bosses (Usually about the future/plans)
want, need, plan, decide, hope, promise
I plan to travel next year.
She decided to quit her job.
Group B: The "-ing" Bosses (Usually about experiences/habits)
enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, suggest, practice
I enjoy reading before bed.
He avoided answering the question.
3) Meaning (what it means) There is no perfect logical rule here, but generally: verbs looking forward to an uncompleted action take to. Verbs dealing with ongoing, real, or finished experiences take -ing.
4) Use (when to choose it)
At the A2 level, this is purely an exercise in memorizing high-frequency combinations so you can speak without pausing to translate.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Wrong: I enjoy to cook.
Right: I enjoy cooking.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Defaulting to the infinitive for everything (I finished to eat / I avoid to drive).
Fix: Memorize Group B (enjoy, avoid, finish, mind) immediately. These are the most common troublemakers.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, chaining two verbs almost always uses the infinitive (Terminei de comer, evito dirigir, gosto de ler). The English gerund (-ing) feels unnatural because it looks like a continuous action. Remind learners that the gerund is acting as a noun (the concept of the activity), not a continuous verb.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I don't want to do that. → Upgrade: I'd prefer not to do that.
Starter: Do you care if I sit here? → Upgrade: Do you mind if I sit here? / Would you mind moving over?
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose the correct form.

Guided: Finish the sentences truly.

A2.G.10. Articles II
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Tell a cohesive story by tracking the flow of information.
Specify exactly which object you want someone to interact with.
Make general statements about the world.
2) Form (how to build it)
The Core Rule: New vs. Known Information
A / An: Use when mentioning a singular countable noun for the first time. (It's new to the listener).
The: Use when mentioning it for the second time, OR if there is only one in the room/world. (It's known to the listener).
The Zero Article (Ø) Rule:
Use no article for plural nouns or noncount nouns when speaking in general.
3) Meaning (what it means) Articles are the spotlight of English grammar. "A/An" turns the spotlight on a random item in a crowded room. "The" narrows the spotlight onto one specific, agreed-upon item.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Storytelling flow: "I saw a man and a dog. The man was angry, but the dog was happy." (First mention = A. Second mention = The).
The "Only One" rule: "Can you close the door?" (There is only one door in this room. We both know which one it is).
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
A vs. The
Let's watch a movie. (Any movie, we haven't decided yet).
Let's watch the movie. (The specific one we talked about earlier).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using "the" for general categories (The women live longer than the men / The nature is beautiful).
Fix: If you mean 100% of the group in the whole world, drop the article entirely. (Women live longer / Nature is beautiful).
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese requires definite articles for broad concepts (A natureza, as mulheres, a sociedade, o amor). This is the single most frequent article error for Brazilian adults. Drill the "Zero Article for General Truths" rule aggressively.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I have a question about the grammar. → Upgrade: I have a question about this specific grammar point.
Starter: The life is hard. → Upgrade: Life can be tough sometimes.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose a/an, the, or zero article (Ø).
I bought ___________ new laptop yesterday. ___________ laptop is very fast.

Could you pass me ___________ salt, please? (Hint: It's on the table between us).
Guided: Fill in the blanks.
I think ___________ money is important, but it isn't everything.
I work in ___________ office. ___________ office is located downtown.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write a three-sentence story about finding an object. Use "a/an" the first time you mention the object, and "the" the next two times.

A2.G.11. Quantifiers (expanded)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Estimate crowd sizes, budgets, and remaining time.
Complain about having too much work or not enough resources.
Give general data without needing exact numbers.
2) Form (how to build it)
Large Amounts
A lot of / Lots of: Used for both Count and Noncount nouns. (A lot of cars / A lot of water).
Much: Noncount nouns. (Mostly in questions/negatives: I don't have much time).
Many: Plural Count nouns. (Mostly in questions/negatives: Are there many people?)
Small Amounts
A few: Plural Count nouns. (I have a few friends).
A little: Noncount nouns. (I have a little money).
3) Meaning (what it means)
A lot of is the universal, friendly affirmative quantifier.
A few / A little mean "some, but not a lot." They have a positive feeling (it's enough to survive).
4) Use (when to choose it)
In modern spoken English, native speakers rarely use much or many in affirmative sentences. (Saying "I have much money" sounds strange). Always default to a lot of for affirmative sentences.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
A few vs. Few (The article changes the feeling)
I have a few friends. (Positive: I have 3 or 4. I am happy).
I have few friends. (Negative: I only have 1 or 2. I am lonely).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Mixing count/noncount quantifiers (I have much cars / There is many traffic).
Fix: Memorize the split: Many/Few = Countable. Much/Little = Uncountable.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, muito/muitos works for everything (muito trânsito, muitos carros). Furthermore, muita gente (a lot of people) is singular in Portuguese. Brazilians frequently say "much people" or "many persons." Drill that people is the irregular plural of person and takes many or a lot of.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I don't have much time. → Upgrade: I'm a little short on time.
Starter: There are a lot of people here. → Upgrade: It's pretty crowded in here.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose the correct quantifier.
How (much / many) luggage are you taking on the trip?

Guided: Finish the sentence based on your life.

A2.G.12. Count/noncount II
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Order food accurately in restaurants or at the grocery store.
Pack luggage and organize supplies.
Break abstract concepts down into manageable pieces.
2) Form (how to build it)
You cannot put a number directly in front of a noncount noun (e.g., two waters). You must use a Measure Word + of.
Liquids & Foods
a bottle / glass / cup of water
a piece / slice of cake
a loaf of bread
a can of soda
Abstract Concepts (The tricky ones)
a piece of advice
a piece of information
an item of news
a piece of furniture
3) Meaning (what it means) Measure words act like containers. They take a continuous, unshaped substance (like water or advice) and package it into a box so you can put a number on it.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use these when you need exact quantities. If you don't need exact quantities, just use some (A1.G.05).
Spoken exception: In a cafe, it is acceptable to say "Two coffees, please," because everyone understands you mean "Two cups of coffee."
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Wrong: Please give me an advice.
Right: Please give me a piece of advice. (Or just: Please give me some advice).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Trying to pluralize the noncount noun instead of the measure word (Two bottles of waters / Three pieces of informations).
Fix: The noncount noun NEVER gets an 's'. Only the container gets the 's' (Two bottles of water).
L1 Transfer Note: As noted in A1, Portuguese makes these words plural (informações, conselhos, móveis, pães). The concept of a "piece of advice" or a "loaf of bread" feels overly wordy. Emphasize that failing to use these measure words is one of the quickest ways to sound non-native.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I need a paper. → Upgrade: Could I get a blank sheet of paper?
Starter: She gave me a good advice. → Upgrade: She gave me a great piece of advice. / She gave me some solid advice.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Match the measure word to the noncount noun.
a slice of (a) water

Guided: What container or measure word would you use to buy these?
milk: a ____________________ of milk
chocolate: a ____________________ of chocolate

A2.G.13. Comparatives & modifiers (a bit, much, far; not as…as)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Explain exactly how much better or worse an option is.
Show that two things are equal (or unequal).
Soften a comparison so you don't sound too aggressive.
2) Form (how to build it)
Modifiers with Comparatives (Reviewing A1.G.15)
Big difference: much / far / a lot + comparative (e.g., much bigger, far more expensive)
Small difference: a bit / a little + comparative (e.g., a bit colder, a little faster)
Equality / Inequality: (not) as... as
Equal (=): Subject + be + as + adjective + as + Object
My car is as fast as yours.
Unequal (≠): Subject + be + not as + adjective + as + Object
The sequel was not as good as the first movie.
3) Meaning (what it means) Modifiers act like a volume knob for your comparatives, turning the intensity up or down. The as...as structure allows you to compare things without using the -er or more forms at all.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Using not as...as is a very diplomatic way to say something is worse. Saying "The hotel was not as clean as the pictures" is much softer and more professional than saying "The hotel was dirtier than the pictures."
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Direct Comparative: A BMW is more expensive than a Ford.
Not as...as: A Ford is not as expensive as a BMW. (These mean exactly the same thing, just from different angles).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Mixing modifiers with the base adjective (It is much expensive / It is far good).
Fix: Modifiers like much and far MUST sit next to a comparative form (much more expensive / far better).
Error: Saying "so...as" instead of "as...as" (She is not so tall as him).
Fix: While you might read "so...as" in very old books, in modern English, always use as...as for both affirmative and negative sentences.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the structure tão... quanto maps perfectly to as... as. The main issue Brazilians have is translating muito mais directly as very more instead of much more. Drill the chunk: much more / much better.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: The test was harder than I thought. → Upgrade: The test was much harder than I expected.
Starter: This is worse than the old one. → Upgrade: This is nowhere near as good as the old one.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Rewrite the sentence using not as... as.
Trains are faster than buses. → Buses are ____________________ trains.
Silver is cheaper than gold. → Silver is ____________________ gold.
Guided: Compare these items using a modifier (a bit, much) or as...as.

A2.G.14. Relative clauses (subject/object)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Speak more fluently by linking short sentences together.
Clearly identify which person or thing you are talking about.
Sound more like a native speaker by dropping unnecessary words.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject Relative Clauses (The pronoun does the action)
The pronoun (who/that) cannot be dropped.
The man [who called me] was angry. (The man called).
I want a phone [that takes good photos]. (The phone takes photos).
Object Relative Clauses (The pronoun receives the action)
The pronoun (who/that) CAN be dropped (and usually is!).
The man [(who) I called] was angry. (I called the man).
The phone [(that) I bought] is great. (I bought the phone).
3) Meaning (what it means) Relative clauses define a noun. When the noun is the object of the clause (e.g., "the book I read"), the relative pronoun becomes "invisible grammar" in spoken English.
4) Use (when to choose it)
At the A2 level, dropping the object relative pronoun is mainly receptive. You need to recognize it when listening to native speakers, who drop that and who almost constantly.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Written/Formal: The report that you sent is excellent.
Spoken/Natural: The report you sent is excellent.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting the object pronoun back in at the end of the clause (The book that I read it was good).
Fix: That already replaces it. You can't have both.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the word que (that/who) is absolutely mandatory to connect these ideas (O livro que eu li). Dropping it feels incredibly uncomfortable for Brazilians. Challenge them to practice reading sentences aloud while completely skipping the word that. It will feel weird, but it sounds highly fluent.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: This is the laptop that I use. → Upgrade: This is the laptop I use.
Starter: The person who I met was nice. → Upgrade: The person I spoke with was really helpful.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Can you drop the pronoun (who/that) in this sentence? (Yes/No).
I like the girl who sits next to me. (___)

Guided: Combine the sentences by dropping the relative pronoun.

A2.G.15. Basic passive (present/past)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Describe where products come from and how they are made.
Talk about historical events and monuments.
Report news or facts without knowing exactly who did the action.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + be (am/is/are/was/were) + Past Participle (V3)
Present Passive (Routines / Facts)
Active: They speak Portuguese in Brazil.
Passive: Portuguese is spoken in Brazil.
Active: They make these phones in China.
Passive: These phones are made in China.
Past Passive (History / Finished Events)
Active: Someone stole my wallet.
Passive: My wallet was stolen.
Active: Leonardo da Vinci painted the Mona Lisa.
Passive: The Mona Lisa was painted by Leonardo da Vinci.
3) Meaning (what it means) The passive voice changes the camera angle. Instead of looking at the person doing the action, the camera points directly at the thing receiving the action.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use the passive when the "doer" is unknown, unimportant, or obvious. (e.g., The thief was arrested. It's obvious the police arrested him, so we don't need to say "by the police").
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Active: Someone built this house in 1950. (Focus is on the mysterious builder).
Passive: This house was built in 1950. (Focus is on the house itself).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Forgetting the verb be entirely (The book written by Tolkien / The car made in Germany).
Fix: A passive sentence without be is like a car without an engine. It goes nowhere. You MUST have is/are or was/were before the V3.
L1 Transfer Note: Brazilians understand the passive well because it exists in Portuguese (foi construído, é feito), but they often default to the active voice using the third-person plural (Construíram esta casa / Fizeram este carro). Encourage them to use the English passive for these "anonymous they" situations, as it sounds much more natural.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: People started the company in 2010. → Upgrade: The company was founded in 2010.
Starter: Where do they make this? → Upgrade: Where is this manufactured?
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose Present Passive or Past Passive.
The Harry Potter books (are written / were written) by J.K. Rowling.
Millions of emails (are sent / were sent) every single day.
My car (is repaired / was repaired) last week.
Guided: Change the active sentences to passive.
Someone cleans the office every night. → The office ____________________.
Someone broke the window yesterday. → The window ____________________.
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: Think of a famous building, food, or product from your city. Tell a partner two passive facts about it (e.g., where it is made, when it was created).

A2.G.16. Reported speech (intro: say/tell)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Pass on a message from a boss or a client.
Gossip about what someone said.
Remind someone of a promise they made.
2) Form (how to build it)
The Core Verbs: Say vs. Tell
Say: Subject + said + (that) + Sentence.
He said (that) he was tired.
Tell: Subject + told + PERSON + (that) + Sentence.
He told me (that) he was tired.
Basic Tense Shifting (Backshifting) When you report what someone said in the past, their original words usually take one step back in time.
Present Simple → Past Simple: "I want coffee." → She said she wanted coffee.
Present Continuous → Past Continuous: "I am working." → He said he was working.
Will → Would: "I will call you." → They said they would call me.
3) Meaning (what it means) You are acting as a messenger. Because time has passed since the person originally spoke, you shift the verbs backwards into the past to show that the quote belongs to history, not right now.
4) Use (when to choose it)
In spoken English, you can completely ignore the word that. (She said she was busy).
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Direct Quote: John said, "I am hungry." (You are reading a script).
Reported: John said he was hungry. (You are summarizing the message).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Forgetting the person after tell, or adding a person immediately after say (He told that... / He said me that...).
Fix: Tell needs a target (told me, told him, told Sarah). Say shoots the message into the air (said that).
L1 Transfer Note: As mentioned previously, Portuguese allows Ele me disse (He said me) and Ele contou que (He told that). You must break this habit early. Drill the patterns: Said that / Told me that.
Error: Not shifting the tense (She said she is tired).
Fix: While native speakers sometimes don't shift the tense if the fact is still true right now, it is safer and grammatically stronger for A2 learners to always shift the verb to the past.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: He said to me that he will come. → Upgrade: He told me he would come.
Starter: The boss said we need to finish. → Upgrade: The boss mentioned we needed to finish.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose said or told.
My manager ___________ me to take the day off.
The client ___________ the price was too high.
I ___________ them that I would be late.
Guided: Change the direct quote into reported speech.
"I am living in London." → She said she ____________________.
"We will finish the project." → They told us they ____________________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Think of a meeting or conversation you had recently. Write two sentences reporting what the other person told you using said and told me.

A2.G.17. Requests and politeness structures (A2→B1 bridge)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Ask for favors without sounding rude or demanding.
Offer food, drinks, or help to guests and clients.
Function smoothly in a professional English environment.
2) Form (how to build it)
Making Requests (Asking someone to do something)
Could you + Base Verb + please?
Could you send me the file, please?
Would you mind + Verb-ing? (Advanced/Very polite)
Would you mind sending me the file?
Making Offers (Asking if someone wants something)
Would you like + Noun?
Would you like a coffee?
Would you like me to + Base Verb?
Would you like me to call a taxi?
3) Meaning (what it means) These structures use modal verbs in the past tense (could, would) NOT to talk about the past, but to create "polite distance." They soften your words so you do not sound like a dictator giving orders.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use Could you...? with coworkers, strangers, and in professional emails.
Never use I want in a restaurant or cafe. Always use I would like (I'd like).
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Aggressive: Send me the report. / I want a coffee.
Polite: Could you send me the report? / I'd like a coffee, please.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Direct translation of requests (Can you to help me? / Do you want a coffee?).
Fix: Drop the to after could. Use Would you like instead of Do you want.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, Você quer um café? is perfectly polite. In English, "Do you want a coffee?" sounds very direct, almost like you are challenging them. Train learners to automatically use Would you like in hospitality or office settings. Similarly, Brazilians often use the imperative to ask favors (Me manda o arquivo). English requires the modal wrapper (Could you send...).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I want the chicken, please. → Upgrade: I'll have the chicken, please. / I'd like the chicken.
Starter: Do you want help? → Upgrade: Can I give you a hand with that?
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Find and correct the mistake.
Could you to open the window, please?
Do you want that I call the manager?
Would you mind to wait for a moment?
Guided: Turn these direct statements into polite requests or offers.
Give me some water. → ____________________?
Do you want a piece of cake? → ____________________?

A2.G.18. Adverbs & intensifiers (really/very/quite; well vs good)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Add emotion, emphasis, and detail to your opinions.
Evaluate someone's performance or a product's quality accurately.
Modulate your tone (from "it's okay" to "it's amazing").
2) Form (how to build it)
Intensifiers (Before Adjectives)
Quite / Pretty: 70% intensity. (The movie was quite good. I liked it.)
Very: 90% intensity. (The movie was very good. Highly recommended.)
Really: 100% intensity. (The movie was really fantastic!)
Good vs. Well (The Ultimate Rule)
Good is an adjective. It describes Nouns (Things/People).
He is a good player. / This is a good pizza.
Well is an adverb. It describes Verbs (Actions).
He plays well. / The team performed well.
3) Meaning (what it means) Intensifiers control the volume of your adjectives. "Well" and "Good" are tools to make sure you are describing the correct part of the sentence (the object vs. the action).
4) Use (when to choose it)
Pretty is extremely common in spoken American English to mean "quite" (It's pretty expensive = It's quite expensive). It has nothing to do with beauty in this context.
Exception: When talking about your health, use well as an adjective. ("I don't feel well").
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Wrong: He speaks English very good. (Describing the action 'speaks' with an adjective).
Right: He speaks English very well. (Describing the action with an adverb).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using very with strong adjectives (The movie was very fantastic / very terrible).
Fix: Very is for normal adjectives (very good, very bad). If the adjective is already at 100% (fantastic, terrible, huge), use really or absolutely.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, bem and bom are sometimes swapped in casual speech, and muito covers all intensifiers. The "He speaks very good" error is universal among Brazilian learners. Drill the connection: Action = Well.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: It is very good. → Upgrade: It's pretty decent. / It's really outstanding.
Starter: I did a good job. → Upgrade: I think the presentation went really well.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose good or well.
Did you sleep ___________ last night?
That is a very ___________ idea.
She manages the marketing team extremely ___________.
Guided: Add an intensifier (quite, very, really) based on the cue.
(70%) The new restaurant is ___________ expensive, but not too bad.
(100%) I was ___________ exhausted after the 12-hour flight.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write two sentences reviewing a product you bought recently. Describe the product itself using good/very, and describe how it performs/works using well.

A2.G.19. Prepositions after verbs/adjectives (starter sets)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Talk about your hobbies and skills accurately.
Express interest or disinterest in various topics.
Sound more natural when pairing action words with objects.
2) Form (how to build it)
Some verbs and adjectives must always be followed by a specific preposition.
Adjective + Preposition
good / bad at (math, sports, cooking)
interested in (history, art)
afraid of (spiders, heights)
Verb + Preposition
listen to (music, a podcast)
depend on (the weather, the price)
wait for (a friend, the bus)
ask for (help, the bill)
3) Meaning (what it means) These prepositions don't use their normal "location" meanings (like in the box). They act as invisible glue that attaches the adjective or verb to the noun that follows it.
4) Use (when to choose it)
If a verb comes directly after these prepositions, you MUST add -ing to it (e.g., I am good at playing chess).
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Wrong: I listen music. (Missing the glue).
Right: I listen to music.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Directly translating the preposition from the native language (I am good in math / It depends of the price).
Fix: Treat the verb/adjective and its preposition as a single vocabulary word. Never learn "depend"; only learn "depend on."
L1 Transfer Note: Brazilian learners universally struggle here because Portuguese uses different prepositions (bom em = good in, depende de = depends of). Drill these starter sets heavily, especially listen to and good at, as they are used constantly in casual conversation.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I like history. → Upgrade: I'm really interested in history.
Starter: I am bad at cooking. → Upgrade: I'm terrible at cooking.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose the correct preposition.
Are you listening (at / to / for) me?

Guided: Complete the sentences so they are true for you.

A2.G.20. Question forms expansion (indirect questions controlled)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Politely ask for directions or information from strangers.
Soften questions in a professional or service environment.
Avoid sounding like an interrogator.
2) Form (how to build it)
Introductory Phrase + Question Word + Subject + Verb
Direct: Where is the station?
Indirect: Can you tell me where the station is?
Direct: What time does the club start?
Indirect: Do you know what time the club starts?
3) Meaning (what it means) By adding "Can you tell me..." or "Do you know...", you change the focus. You are politely asking if the person has the information, rather than directly demanding the information itself.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use this whenever you approach someone you don't know well, like a receptionist, a stranger on the street, or a new client.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Direct (Aggressive to strangers): Where is the bathroom?
Indirect (Polite): Excuse me, do you know where the bathroom is?
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Keeping the question word order in the second half of the sentence (Can you tell me where is the bank?).
Fix: The first part (Can you tell me) does the heavy lifting for the question. The second part must return to normal statement order (Subject -> Verb).
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the word order does not change at all (Onde é o banco? / Você sabe onde é o banco?). This syntactic flip requires a lot of controlled practice before it feels natural to a Brazilian learner.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: What time is it? → Upgrade: Do you happen to know what time it is?
Starter: How much is this? → Upgrade: Could you tell me how much this costs?

A2.G.21. Connecting ideas beyond because (although, however)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Contrast two different ideas or express surprise.
Make your spoken and written English flow more smoothly.
Introduce a problem politely before offering a solution.
2) Form (how to build it)
Although (Linking two ideas in ONE sentence)
Although it was raining, we played soccer.
We played soccer although it was raining.
(Rule: Never use "but" in the same sentence as "although").
However (Connecting TWO separate sentences)
It was raining. However, we played soccer.
(Rule: Always use a period or semicolon before it, and a comma after it).
3) Meaning (what it means) Both words show a contrast or an unexpected result. They signal to the listener: "I am about to say something that goes against what you expect."
4) Use (when to choose it)
These words instantly make your English sound more advanced and organized than just saying but all the time.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Basic: I am tired, but I have to work.
Expanded: Although I am tired, I have to work.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Joining two sentences with however using only commas (It is expensive, however, I like it).
Fix: However is too heavy for just a comma. It needs a full stop (.) before it.
L1 Transfer Note: Brazilians often use embora (although) correctly, but struggle with the punctuation of however because porém is punctuated very loosely in informal Portuguese writing.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I like it but it's expensive. → Upgrade: I really like it. However, it's a bit out of my budget.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose Although or However.
___________ I studied hard, I didn't pass the exam.
The restaurant has great food. ___________, the service is very slow.
Guided: Finish the sentence logically.
Although I woke up late today, ____________________.

A2.G.22. Pronouns: one/ones; it/this/that (reference)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Point out specific items in a store without repeating the name of the item.
Speak faster and more efficiently.
Avoid sounding repetitive.
2) Form (how to build it)
Replacing singular countable nouns: ONE
"Which phone do you want?" → "I want the black one." (Replacing phone).
Replacing plural countable nouns: ONES
"Which shoes are yours?" → "The brown ones." (Replacing shoes).
Pointing at things (Demonstratives)
It: Refers to a specific thing previously mentioned. ("Where is my book? It is on the table.")
This/That: Used to point at things physically or point back to a whole idea. ("I passed my test! That is great news!")
3) Meaning (what it means) These words act as placeholders. Once you establish what you are talking about (e.g., cars, shoes, ideas), you use these pronouns so you don't have to keep saying the noun over and over.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use one/ones primarily when you are choosing between different options in a category (e.g., shopping, picking a seat).
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Repetitive: I don't like the red shirt. I prefer the blue shirt.
Natural: I don't like the red shirt. I prefer the blue one.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Dropping the pronoun entirely (I prefer the blue / Give me the big).
Fix: English adjectives usually cannot stand alone. You must attach them to a noun or the placeholder one.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, it is perfectly natural to drop the noun and just use the adjective (Eu prefiro o azul). Point out that saying "I prefer the blue" sounds unfinished to an English speaker.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Which car is yours? The red car. → Upgrade: Mine is the red one over there.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Complete the response using one or ones.
"Do you want the large coffee or the small ___________?"

Guided: Respond to the question without repeating the noun.
"Which jacket do you want to wear today? (the warm)" → I think I'll wear ____________________.

A2.G.24. “Used to” (past habits)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Contrast your life now with how it was in the past.
Talk about childhood habits, old hobbies, or previous jobs.
Tell nostalgic stories.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + used to + Base Verb
I used to play video games. (Affirmative)
Negative and Questions (Treat it like regular Past Simple!)
I didn't use to like coffee. (Drop the 'd'!)
Did you use to live here? (Drop the 'd'!)
3) Meaning (what it means) Used to means two things simultaneously:
I did this action regularly in the past.
I do NOT do this action anymore.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use it when you want to highlight a major change in your life or habits.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Past Simple: I played tennis yesterday. (Happened once).
Used to: I used to play tennis. (Habit in the past, but I quit).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Confusing used to (past habit) with usually (present habit). (I used to wake up at 7:00 every day now).
Fix: Used to is exclusively for the past. For present habits, just use the simple present or usually.
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese uses the Pretérito Imperfeito (Eu jogava / Eu morava) to express this idea smoothly. Sometimes learners translate costumava, but often they just stick to the simple past (I played). Encourage the use of used to as it instantly signals to the listener that the habit has stopped.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: In the past, I lived in São Paulo. Now I don't. → Upgrade: I used to live in São Paulo.
Starter: I hated vegetables, but now I like them. → Upgrade: I didn't use to like vegetables.

I didn't used to drink coffee.

Guided: Finish the sentence to show a contrast with your life today.

A2.G.25. Second conditional (intro; polite hypotheticals)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Give safe, non-judgmental advice (e.g., "If I were you...").
Dream about winning the lottery or changing your life.
Answer creative interview questions (e.g., "What would you do if...").
2) Form (how to build it)
If + Subject + Past Simple, Subject + would + Base Verb
If I had more time, I would travel.
If she knew the answer, she would tell us.
The "Were" Rule: In formal English (and common polite advice), we use were for all subjects, including I/he/she/it.
If I were you, I would apply for the job.
3) Meaning (what it means) Despite using the past tense, this grammar talks about the present or the future. It describes a situation that is imaginary, highly unlikely, or currently impossible.
4) Use (when to choose it)
If I were you, I'd... is the standard, polite way to give advice without sounding bossy.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
First Conditional (Real): If I have time later, I will help you. (I probably have time).
Second Conditional (Unreal): If I had time, I would help you. (I am too busy. I can't help).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting would inside the If clause (If I would have money, I would buy it).
Fix: The If clause takes the simple past. Would is only for the result.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the imperfect subjunctive is used (Se eu tivesse). Because English reuses the simple past (If I had), it feels wrong to Brazilians because it doesn't look like a hypothetical "mood." Drill the formula strictly.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: You should quit your job. → Upgrade: If I were you, I'd start looking for a new job.

A2.G.26. Reflexives (myself, yourself)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Explain accidents or injuries clearly.
Emphasize that you did something without any help.
Talk about self-care and independence.
2) Form (how to build it)
I → myself
You (singular) → yourself
He → himself
She → herself
It → itself
We → ourselves
You (plural) → yourselves
They → themselves
3) Meaning (what it means) Reflexive pronouns act like a mirror. You use them when the subject (the doer) and the object (the receiver) are the exact same person.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Mirror actions: I cut myself while cooking. (I did it to me).
Emphasis (By + reflexive): I fixed the car by myself. (Alone, with zero help).
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Object Pronoun: She looked at her. (She looked at a different woman).
Reflexive: She looked at herself. (She looked in the mirror).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using reflexives with verbs of feeling, relaxing, or concentrating (I feel myself sick / I need to concentrate myself).
Fix: In English, these verbs are not reflexive. Just say: I feel sick / I need to concentrate.
L1 Transfer Note: This is a huge trap. Portuguese uses reflexive pronouns constantly (sentir-se, lembrar-se, sentar-se, preocupar-se). Translating these directly ("I remember myself," "I worry myself") sounds incredibly bizarre in English. Teach learners to drop the reflexive for feelings and mental states.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I did this alone. → Upgrade: I managed to finish it all by myself.

A2.G.27. Tag questions (very basic)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Check if someone agrees with your opinion.

Sound friendly and inclusive in a conversation.
2) Form (how to build it)
Positive Statement + Negative Tag
It is a nice day, isn't it?

Negative Statement + Positive Tag
You aren't tired, are you?

(Note: The verb in the tag must match the tense/verb of the main statement. See B1.G.17 for the full expansion).

4) Use (when to choose it)

5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Direct check: Is it a nice day? (You literally don't know).

A2.G.28. Sentence expansion toolkit
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Build longer, more detailed sentences without getting tangled up.
Describe events accurately by detailing how, where, and when they happened.
Ensure your written English looks organized and native-like.
2) Form (how to build it)
When adding details to the end of a sentence, English follows a strict, unwritten rule: SVOMPT.
Subject: Who did it?
Verb: What did they do?
Object: To what/whom?
Manner: How? (adverbs: quickly, well, by bus)
Place: Where? (at the office, in Brazil)
Time: When? (yesterday, at 5:00)
Example: [I] [studied] [English] [quietly] [in my room] [last night].
3) Meaning (what it means) This is the architectural blueprint of an English sentence.
4) Use (when to choose it)
You don't need all the letters every time. But if you have Place and Time, Place always beats Time. (e.g., I arrived in Blumenau yesterday. NOT: I arrived yesterday in Blumenau.)
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Wrong order: I studied last night English quietly in my room.
Correct order: I studied English quietly in my room last night.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting the Time in the middle of the sentence (I went yesterday to the mall).
Fix: Time goes at the very end of the sentence (or sometimes at the very beginning for emphasis). Never in the middle.
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese is much more flexible with word order. You can easily say Eu fui ontem ao shopping. Emphasize that English is rigid. Lock the SVOMPT acronym into their minds as a checklist for proofreading their writing.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I drove to work quickly today. → Upgrade: You can move the Time to the front for variety: Today, I drove to work quickly.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Put the words in the correct SVOMPT order.
(dinner / we / at the restaurant / ate / slowly / yesterday)
(the guitar / he / well / plays)
(to the meeting / she / walked / quickly / this morning)
Guided: Expand this basic sentence: "I drank coffee."
Add a place: ____________________.
Add a time: ____________________.
Add a manner (how): ____________________.

B1.G.01. Present perfect vs past simple (core distinction)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Explain problems that are affecting you right now (e.g., "I've lost my keys").
Share your life experiences and professional background in interviews.
Distinguish between historical facts and recent news.
2) Form (how to build it)
Present Perfect: Subject + have/has + Past Participle (V3)
I have ('ve) finished the report.
She hasn't seen that movie.
Have you been to New York?
Past Simple: Subject + Past Verb (V2) (Review A1.G.11)
I finished the report yesterday.
She didn't see the movie last night.
Did you go to New York in 2015?
3) Meaning (what it means)
Past Simple: The door is closed. The time period is 100% finished (yesterday, last year, when I was a kid).
Present Perfect: The door is open. The action happened in the past, but the time period is not finished (today, this week, in my life), OR the result is highly relevant right now.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Choose Past Simple when you want to answer "When exactly did it happen?"
Choose Present Perfect when the exact time doesn't matter. What matters is the experience or the present result.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Life Experience (Unfinished time vs. Finished time)
My grandfather went to Mexico three times. (He is deceased; his life timeline is finished).
I have been to Mexico three times. (I am alive; my life timeline is still open. I might go a fourth time).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using specific finished time words with the present perfect (I have visited London last year).
Fix: The moment you say "last year," the time is finished. You must switch to Past Simple. (I visited London last year).
Error: Using present simple for actions that started in the past and continue today (I live here for five years).
Fix: Use Present Perfect to connect the past to the present. (I have lived here for five years).
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the present tense is used for unfinished durations (Eu moro aqui há cinco anos). You must aggressively drill that English requires a "bridge" tense (Present Perfect) when combining the past and the present. Furthermore, Portuguese uses the Pretérito Perfeito for both finished actions and life experiences (Eu visitei Londres can mean "I visited" or "I have visited"). Emphasize the concept of "open vs. closed doors" rather than direct translation.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I didn't see him today. → Upgrade: I haven't seen him around today.
Starter: I never eat sushi. → Upgrade: I**'ve never tried** sushi.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose Present Perfect or Past Simple.
I (have lost / lost) my keys yesterday.
Oh no! I (have broken / broke) my phone. It won't turn on.
(Did you ever eat / Have you ever eaten) Thai food?
Guided: Answer the questions.
What is something you have done this week? → I have ____________________.
What is something you did last week? → I ____________________.
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: You are in a job interview. Tell the interviewer about two major projects you have completed in your career, and then give a specific past detail about one of them (e.g., "I have managed a large team. In 2022, I hired 50 new employees...").

B1.G.02. Present perfect continuous
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Explain why you look tired, sweaty, or stressed right now.
Talk about how long you have been doing an activity.
Complain about ongoing issues.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + have/has + been + Verb-ing
Affirmative (+)
Negative (-)
Question (?)
I have been waiting.
I haven't been waiting.
Have you been waiting?
She has been working.
She hasn't been working.
Has she been working?

3) Meaning (what it means)
This tense emphasizes the duration and the continuous nature of an activity that started in the past and is either still happening now or has just recently stopped, leaving a visible result.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Focus on Duration: Use it with for and since to emphasize how long something has been happening. (I've been studying for three hours).
Focus on Visible Evidence: If someone walks in breathing heavily, you ask: "Have you been running?" The action might be over, but the physical evidence is present.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Present Perfect Simple vs. Present Perfect Continuous
I have painted the living room. (Focus on the finished result. The job is done).
I have been painting the living room. (Focus on the activity. My clothes have wet paint on them, and the job might not be finished yet).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using state verbs in the continuous form (I have been knowing him for years).
Fix: State verbs (know, like, believe, understand) rarely take the -ing form. Use the Present Perfect Simple instead. (I have known him for years).
Error: Using present continuous for duration (I am waiting for you for two hours).
Fix: The present continuous only cares about right exactly now. If you add duration (for two hours), you must use the Present Perfect Continuous. (I have been waiting for two hours).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I work on this project a lot recently. → Upgrade: I**'ve been spending a lot of time on** this project lately.
Starter: Sorry I am late. I waited for the bus. → Upgrade: Sorry I'm late. I**'ve been waiting** for the bus for ages.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Complete the sentence with the Present Perfect Continuous.
Your eyes are red. ___________ you ___________ (cry)?
I ___________ (try) to call you all morning! Where were you?
They ___________ (live) in that house since 2010.
Guided: Look at the situations. Write a sentence explaining the visible evidence.
The ground outside is wet. → It ____________________.
Your coworker looks exhausted. → They ____________________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write a text message to a friend apologizing for not replying sooner. Explain what busy activity you have been doing all day that kept you away from your phone.

B1.G.03. Narrative tenses (storytelling toolkit)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Tell engaging, dynamic stories about your past.
Write incident reports or explain how an accident happened.
Set the scene before delivering the main plot points.
2) Form (how to build it)
This is a combination of three tenses working together:
The Background: Past Continuous (was/were + -ing)
The Main Events: Past Simple (V2)
The Backstory: Past Perfect (had + V3) (See B1.G.04 for deep dive)
3) Meaning (what it means)
Good storytelling isn't just a list of facts. Narrative tenses act like a camera. The Past Continuous paints the background scenery. The Past Simple is the action moving the plot forward. The Past Perfect is a flashback.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Setting the scene: "The sun was shining, and the birds were singing..."
The interrupting action: "...when suddenly, a dog ran across the street."
Providing context/flashback: "I recognized the dog because I had seen it the day before."
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Past Simple vs. Past Continuous (Sequence vs. Overlap)
When I arrived, they ate dinner. (I arrived first. Then, we all started eating together).
When I arrived, they were eating dinner. (They started eating before I arrived. I interrupted their meal).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Overusing the past continuous for main events (I was waking up, and then I was taking a shower).
Fix: Use the past continuous for the "vibe" or the interrupted background action. For the chronological steps of the story, stick to the simple past.
L1 Transfer Note: Brazilian learners intuitively grasp the Past Continuous well because it maps cleanly to the Pretérito Imperfeito for background descriptions (O sol brilhava / estava brilhando). The main issue is remembering to drop back into the simple past for the hard plot points.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I drove my car. I saw an accident. → Upgrade: While I was driving, I saw an accident.
Starter: I walked to work. It started raining. → Upgrade: I was walking to work when it suddenly started raining.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose the correct narrative tense.
I (walked / was walking) home when I (found / was finding) $20 on the street.
We (slept / were sleeping) soundly until the alarm (went / was going) off.
When the boss walked in, everyone (worked / was working) quietly at their desks.
Guided: Combine the two ideas into one sentence using when or while.
I was cooking dinner. The phone rang. → ____________________.
We were watching TV. The power went out. → ____________________.
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: Think of a time you had a minor accident or lost something important. Tell a partner the story, making sure to describe the background of what you were doing before the bad thing happened.

B1.G.04. Past perfect (basic)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Clarify the timeline of events when telling a complex story.
Give excuses or explain why a problem occurred.
Express regrets about things you didn't do.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + had + Past Participle (V3)
Affirmative (+)
Negative (-)
Question (?)
I had ('d) left.
I had not (hadn't) left.
Had you left?
She had ('d) eaten.
She hadn't eaten.
Had she eaten?

3) Meaning (what it means)
The past perfect is the "past of the past." If you have two finished actions in a story, the past perfect tells the listener which one happened first, even if you talk about it second.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use it when narrating a story in the simple past, and you need to refer to something that happened even earlier.
Common triggers: by the time, already, before, after.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Timeline Clarity
When we arrived at the cinema, the movie started. (We arrived exactly on time. We sat down, and the movie began).
When we arrived at the cinema, the movie had started. (We were late. The movie began before we arrived).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using past perfect for a single event in the past (I had lived in Brazil in 2010).
Fix: If there is only one past event, just use the past simple. The past perfect only exists to show the relationship between two past events.
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese has the Mais-Que-Perfeito (Eu tinha falado / Eu falara), which maps perfectly to the English Past Perfect. Leverage this! If they can say "Eu já tinha comido quando ele chegou," they can say "I had already eaten when he arrived."
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I didn't eat because I ate before. → Upgrade: I wasn't hungry because I had already eaten.
Starter: I realized my mistake later. → Upgrade: By the time I realized my mistake, it had already been too late.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Complete the sentence with the Past Perfect.
I couldn't buy the coffee because I ___________ (forget) my wallet at home.
By the time we got to the station, the train ___________ (leave).
She didn't recognize him because he ___________ (change) so much.
Guided: Connect the two events using the Past Perfect for the older event.
Event 1: I finished my work. Event 2: I went to the party.
→ By the time I went to the party, ____________________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write a short excuse to your boss explaining why you missed an important morning meeting using the past perfect (e.g., "When I woke up, my alarm had...").

B1.G.05. Future continuous / future perfect (receptive)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Understand strict project deadlines and schedules.
Project yourself into the future to imagine a scenario.
Politely ask about people's upcoming plans.
2) Form (how to build it)
Future Continuous: Subject + will be + Verb-ing
This time tomorrow, I will be flying to London.
Will you be using the conference room at 3:00?
Future Perfect: Subject + will have + Past Participle (V3)
By Friday, I will have finished the presentation.
They won't have arrived by 6:00.
3) Meaning (what it means)
Future Continuous: Imagine freezing time at a specific moment in the future. What action is in the middle of happening?
Future Perfect: Imagine standing at a specific point in the future and looking backwards. What action is already 100% completed?
4) Use (when to choose it)
Future Continuous is highly useful in professional settings to soften questions about plans. "Will you be working late tonight?" sounds less demanding than "Are you going to work late?"
Future Perfect is heavily tied to the preposition by (meaning "not later than"). "By the end of the year, we will have launched the product."
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Future Simple vs. Future Continuous
I will call you at 8:00. (I will pick up the phone and dial the number exactly at 8:00).
I will be calling clients at 8:00. (I will start earlier. At exactly 8:00, I will be in the middle of a phone call).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Struggling to construct the long verb phrases (I will have finish / I will be go).
Fix: Break it down into chunks. Will have + V3. Will be + -ing.
Note: At the B1 level, these tenses are largely receptive. Focus on understanding them when reading emails or listening to native speakers, rather than forcing perfect production immediately.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I will finish this before Friday. → Upgrade: I will have this wrapped up by Friday.
Starter: What are your plans for tomorrow at 10? → Upgrade: What will you be doing tomorrow around 10?
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Identify if the sentence is Future Continuous (FC) or Future Perfect (FP).
By next year, she will have graduated from university. (___)
Don't call me at 7:00. I'll be eating dinner. (___)
Will you be needing the car tomorrow? (___)
Guided: Complete the sentences so they make sense for your life.
This time tomorrow, I will be ____________________.
By the end of this year, I will have ____________________.

B1.G.06. Reported speech (statements + questions)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Pass on messages from coworkers or clients.
Gossip or recount interesting conversations you had.
Summarize what was agreed upon in a meeting.
2) Form (how to build it)
Rule 1: Shift the Tense Backwards (Backshifting)
If the reporting verb is in the past (e.g., He said), the quote moves one step back in time.
Present Simple → Past Simple (I like tea → He said he liked tea)
Present Continuous → Past Continuous (I am working → She said she was working)
Past Simple → Past Perfect (I went home → He said he had gone home)
Will → Would (I will call → She said she would call)
Rule 2: Statements vs. Questions
Statements: He said (that) he was tired.
Questions (Yes/No): She asked me if I was tired.
Questions (Wh-): They asked me where I lived. (Note: Regular sentence word order! Not "where did I live")
3) Meaning (what it means)
Reported speech creates distance. You are not just repeating words like a parrot; you are processing the information and conveying the meaning of what someone else said at an earlier time.
4) Use (when to choose it)
The word that is completely optional in statements. (She said that she was leaving = She said she was leaving). In spoken English, we almost always drop it.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Say vs. Tell (The Golden Rule)
Tell must have a person immediately after it (Tell me, Tell John, Tell them).
Say does not take a person directly after it.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using "say to me" or just "tell" without an object (He said me that... / He told that...).
Fix: Memorize this: He said that... OR He told me that... * L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, dizer (say) and contar (tell) operate differently. You can easily say Ele me disse (He said me). This direct translation causes huge errors in English. Drill the "Tell + Person" rule constantly.
Error: Keeping question word order in reported questions (She asked me where did I live).
Fix: Once a question is reported, it is no longer a real question. It becomes a statement. Drop the do/does/did and use normal subject-verb order.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: He said, "I will be late." → Upgrade: He mentioned that he'd be late.
Starter: She asked, "Do you need help?" → Upgrade: She wanted to know if I needed a hand.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose said or told.
She ___________ me that she was moving to Canada.
The manager ___________ the meeting was canceled.
I ___________ him to be careful.
Guided: Change the direct quotes into reported speech.
John: "I am very busy." → John said that he ____________________.
Mary: "Where is the station?" → Mary asked me where ____________________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Think of a piece of advice or an instruction someone gave you recently. Write two sentences reporting what they said and what you replied.

B1.G.07. Passive voice (expanded)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Describe processes (how things are made or done).
Write formal emails or reports where the action is more important than the person doing it.
Tactfully report mistakes without blaming specific people.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + be (in the correct tense) + Past Participle (V3)
Tense
Active
Passive
Present Simple
They make cars here.
Cars are made here.
Past Simple
Someone stole my phone.
My phone was stolen.
Present Perfect
They have fixed the bug.
The bug has been fixed.
Future (will)
We will send the email.
The email will be sent.

Adding the agent: If you need to say who did it, use by (The book was written by George Orwell).
3) Meaning (what it means)
The passive voice flips the focus of a sentence. Instead of focusing on the "doer" (the active subject), it shines a spotlight on the "receiver" (the object).
4) Use (when to choose it)
When the doer is unknown: My bike was stolen. (I don't know who took it).
When the doer is obvious: He was arrested. (Obviously, by the police).
When you want to be diplomatic: A mistake was made. (Instead of "You made a mistake").
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Active vs. Passive focus
Active: A dog bit John. (Focus is on the aggressive dog).
Passive: John was bitten by a dog. (Focus is on poor John).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Forgetting the verb be entirely (The house built in 1990).
Fix: The passive voice cannot exist without be. It carries the tense. (The house was built in 1990).
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese has a similar passive structure (foi construído), but English uses the passive voice much more frequently in everyday situations (e.g., "I was told to come here" vs. Me disseram para vir aqui). Encourage learners to use the passive to sound more professional.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Someone broke the printer. → Upgrade: The printer got broken. (Using get instead of be is very common in informal spoken English).
Starter: We will hold the meeting on Friday. → Upgrade: The meeting is scheduled for Friday.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Rewrite the sentences in the passive voice. Do not include the agent if it's unnecessary.
Someone cleans the office every evening. → The office ____________________.
Shakespeare wrote Hamlet. → Hamlet ____________________.

Guided: Finish the sentences about your hometown.

B1.G.08. Modals of deduction (present)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Make educated guesses based on evidence you can see or hear.
Draw logical conclusions in a meeting or discussion.
Express certainty or impossibility.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + Modal + Base Verb
99% Certain (True): He must be at home. (His car is in the driveway).
50% Possible: He might be / could be at work. (It's a Tuesday afternoon).
99% Certain (False): He can't be on vacation. (I just saw him an hour ago).
3) Meaning (what it means)
These are not used for rules or abilities. They are used to express how confident you are about a conclusion you are drawing in the present moment.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use must when you have strong evidence pointing to "Yes."
Use can't when you have strong evidence pointing to "No."
Use might / may / could when it is a 50/50 guess.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Deduction vs. Fact
Fact: She is the boss. (I know this 100%).
Deduction: She must be the boss. (She is wearing a suit and everyone is listening to her).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using "mustn't" for deductions (He is not answering the phone. He mustn't be home).
Fix: Mustn't is ONLY for strict rules (You mustn't smoke). For deductions, the opposite of must is can't. (He can't be home).
L1 Transfer Note: This is a major trap. In Portuguese, it is completely natural to say Ele não deve estar em casa (He must not be home). Translating this directly leads to sounding like you are giving the person an order not to be home. Drill the must vs. can't scale heavily.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I think he is sick. → Upgrade: He must be coming down with something.
Starter: Maybe it is a mistake. → Upgrade: That could just be a typo.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose the correct modal based on the context.
You've been traveling for 14 hours. You (must / can't) be exhausted!
That (must / can't) be Sarah's coat. Hers is blue, and this one is red.
I'm not sure where the files are. They (might / must) be in the bottom drawer.
Guided: Make a deduction for these situations.
The phone is ringing, but nobody is answering. → They must ____________________.
A new sports car is parked in front of your neighbor's house. → It might ____________________.

B1.G.09. Modals of advice and obligation (nuance)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Give strong, urgent advice to a friend or colleague.
Express moral obligations or expectations.
Warn someone about a negative consequence.
2) Form (how to build it)
Should / Ought to: Subject + should / ought to + Base Verb
Had better: Subject + had better ('d better) + Base Verb
Negatives:
You shouldn't do that.
You ought not to do that. (Very formal/rare)
You had better not do that.
3) Meaning (what it means)
Should: General advice. It's a good idea.
Ought to: Same as should, but slightly more formal or implying a moral duty.
Had better: Strong advice with a threat or warning attached. "Do this, or something bad will happen."
4) Use (when to choose it)
Had better is extremely common in spoken English, usually contracted to 'd better (You'd better hurry up). It is used for specific, immediate situations, not general life advice.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Should vs. Had better
You should watch that movie. It's great! (Friendly recommendation).
You had better finish that report before the boss gets back! (Warning: you will be fired if you don't).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Missing the "to" in ought to, or adding "to" after had better (You ought listen / You had better to leave).
Fix: Treat ought to as one single glued-together word. Had better is a pure modal and takes a naked base verb.
Error: Thinking had better is past tense because of the word had (I had better go yesterday).
Fix: Despite the word had, this phrase ONLY refers to the present or the future.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: It is a good idea to leave now. → Upgrade: We**'d better head out** before traffic gets bad.
Starter: You must apologize. → Upgrade: You really ought to apologize to her.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose the best option.
It's raining heavily. You (should / had better) take an umbrella, or you'll ruin your suit.
I think everyone (ought to / had better) learn a second language.
We ('d better not / shouldn't to) wake the baby up.
Guided: Give strong advice with had better for these problems.
Your flight leaves in an hour, and you aren't at the airport. → You'd better ____________________.
Your friend owes a lot of money to the bank. → He'd better ____________________.

B1.G.10. Conditionals 1 and 2 (productive control)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Negotiate terms in a business deal or personal argument.
Make plans based on future variables (weather, money).
Hypothesize about different realities and dream about the future.
2) Form (how to build it)
First Conditional (Real Future)
If + Present Simple, will + Base Verb
If it rains, we will cancel the match.
We will cancel the match if it rains.
Second Conditional (Unreal Present/Future)
If + Past Simple, would + Base Verb
If I had a million dollars, I would travel the world.
Note on 'Be': In formal English, use were for all subjects (If I were you, If he were here).
3) Meaning (what it means)
1st Conditional: There is a real, realistic chance this condition will happen. (Negotiating reality).
2nd Conditional: The condition is currently impossible, highly unlikely, or purely a fantasy. (Daydreaming or giving advice).
4) Use (when to choose it)
Choose 1st for warnings, threats, and realistic planning.
Choose 2nd for "If I were you..." advice, or to talk about things you wish were different right now.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Real vs. Unreal
Politician A (Confident): "If I win the election, I will lower taxes." (1st Conditional - Real possibility).
Average Citizen: "If I won the election, I would lower taxes." (2nd Conditional - Fantasy; I am not even running for office).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting will or would inside the IF clause (If I will see him, I will tell him).
Fix: If acts like a wall. Will and would cannot go inside the IF clause. They must go in the result clause.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, you use the subjunctive mode for conditionals (Se eu for / Se eu fosse). Because English just reuses the Present Simple and Past Simple, learners often try to insert will to make it sound more like a "future" condition. Drill the formula: IF = Present/Past.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: If you buy this, I will give you a discount. → Upgrade: If you're willing to sign today, we can offer a 10% discount. (Using can instead of will softens it).
Starter: If I were you, I would leave. → Upgrade: If I were in your shoes, I'd walk away.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose 1st or 2nd conditional.
If I (find / found) your wallet, I will call you immediately.
If I (can / could) fly, I would never take a bus again.
What would you do if you (lose / lost) your passport on vacation?
Guided: Finish the conditionals logically.
(1st) If the weather is nice this weekend, I ____________________.
(2nd) If I had more free time, I ____________________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write a short response to a friend who asked for your advice about a difficult coworker. Use "If I were you, I would..."

B1.G.11. Conditionals: unless / as long as / provided that (intro)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Set strict boundaries and rules.
Agree to terms under specific conditions.
Sound more professional in emails and contracts.
2) Form (how to build it)
These words replace "if" in conditional sentences. The grammar rules (1st conditional tense structures) stay the same.
Unless + Affirmative Verb (= If not)
I won't go unless you go. (If you don't go, I won't go).
As long as / Provided that + Clause (= Only if)
You can borrow the car as long as you fill it with gas.
The project will succeed provided that we get funding.
3) Meaning (what it means)
Unless creates an exception to a rule. The action will NOT happen, except in this one specific scenario.
As long as / Provided that creates a strict requirement. The action WILL happen, but only if this specific demand is met.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Unless is great for ultimatums and warnings.
As long as is conversational and friendly for setting conditions.
Provided that is formal, used frequently in business emails, policies, and terms of service.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
If vs. Unless
Let's go for a walk if it doesn't rain.
Let's go for a walk unless it rains. (These mean the exact same thing).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: The double negative with unless (I won't go unless you don't go).
Fix: Unless already means "if not." It has a negative charge built into it. The verb that immediately follows it must be positive.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, a não ser que takes the subjunctive and can sometimes tangle the positive/negative logic for learners translating in their heads. Practice converting "If + Not" sentences into "Unless + Affirmative" sentences.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: You can come if you are quiet. → Upgrade: You can come as long as you keep quiet.
Starter: I will sign it if you change the price. → Upgrade: We are prepared to sign, provided that the price is adjusted.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Rewrite the sentence using unless.
If we don't hurry, we will miss the train. → We will miss the train ____________________.
I won't eat it if it has onions in it. → I won't eat it ____________________.
Guided: Set conditions for these scenarios.
You can use my laptop as long as ____________________.
I won't work on the weekend unless ____________________.
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: Role-play a negotiation with a partner (e.g., buying a used car or agreeing on a deadline). Use as long as to agree to one of their demands, and unless to reject another.

B1.G.12. Gerunds/infinitives (expanded verb sets)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Talk about adopting new habits or quitting bad ones.
Reminisce about past memories or remind people of future tasks.
Describe experiments and problem-solving attempts.
2) Form (how to build it)
Some verbs can be followed by both a gerund (-ing) and an infinitive (to do), but the meaning changes completely.
Set 1: Stop
Stop + -ing: Quit the habit or action forever. (He stopped smoking).
Stop + to do: Interrupt what you are doing in order to do something else. (He was driving, but he stopped to smoke).
Set 2: Remember / Forget
Remember + -ing: A memory from the past. (I remember locking the door. I have a picture of it in my head).
Remember + to do: A task for the future. (Please remember to lock the door when you leave).
Set 3: Try
Try + -ing: Do something as an experiment to see if it solves a problem. (I had a headache, so I tried drinking water. It didn't help).
Try + to do: Attempt something difficult that requires physical or mental effort. (I tried to lift the box, but it was too heavy).
3) Meaning (what it means)
The grammar you choose changes the timeline and the intention of the action. Generally, -ing looks backwards at experiences, and to do looks forward at tasks.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Mastery of these subtle differences is a hallmark of B1/B2 fluency. It allows for precision in storytelling and giving instructions.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
I forgot paying the bill (I paid it, but I lost the memory of doing it. My brain is broken).
I forgot to pay the bill (I didn't pay it. The electricity is turned off).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using stop to do when talking about quitting a bad habit.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, parar de fumar covers quitting the habit, while parar para fumar covers the interruption. Because both use prepositions (de/para), learners often translate both to the infinitive to smoke. Drill the concept that English uses the noun form (-ing) to represent the habit itself.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I must not forget to call him. → Upgrade: I need to make sure I remember to call him.
Starter: I experimented with restarting the computer. → Upgrade: I tried restarting it, but it's still frozen.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose the correct form based on the meaning.
On my way home, I stopped (buying / to buy) some milk.
I'll never forget (meeting / to meet) my favorite musician backstage.
If the software keeps crashing, try (reinstalling / to reinstall) it.
Guided: Complete the sentences truly.
When I was a child, I remember ____________________.
Tomorrow morning, I must remember ____________________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write a short troubleshooting email to an IT desk explaining a problem you are having. Tell them two things you tried doing to fix it, but explain that it didn't work.

B1.G.13. Relative clauses (defining vs non-defining intro)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Add descriptive details to your writing without starting a new sentence.
Provide "by the way" extra information.
Clarify exactly who or what you are talking about.
2) Form (how to build it)
Defining (No Commas = Essential Information)
Tells us which one you mean.
You can use that, who, or which.
The colleagues who work in marketing are having a meeting. (Only the marketing colleagues).
The laptop that I bought yesterday is broken. (A specific laptop).
Non-Defining (Commas = Extra Information)
Adds "by the way" information. The sentence still makes sense without it.
You CANNOT use that. You must use who (people) or which (things).
John**, who** works in marketing**,** is having a meeting. (We already know who John is. His department is just extra info).
My new laptop**, which** I bought yesterday**,** is broken.
3) Meaning (what it means)
Commas act like parentheses ( ). If you put a clause in commas, you are telling the reader: "You can delete this, and the sentence will still be true." If there are no commas, the information is essential to identify the noun.
4) Use (when to choose it)
In spoken English, we often pause slightly where the commas are in a non-defining clause.
In professional emails, non-defining clauses are perfect for slipping in background context (e.g., "The Q3 report, which I attached below, shows our growth.").
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
The "How many brothers?" test
My brother who lives in Paris is a doctor. (Meaning: I have three brothers. I am talking about the Paris one).
My brother, who lives in Paris, is a doctor. (Meaning: I only have one brother. By the way, he lives in Paris).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using "that" after a comma (My boss, that is from Canada, is very strict).
Fix: That is allergic to commas. Switch to who or which.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, que is used universally, and commas are often placed more loosely based on breathing pauses rather than strict grammatical rules. Drill the "That = No Commas" rule heavily.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I spoke to Sarah. She is the manager. → Upgrade: I spoke to Sarah, who is the manager.
Starter: The document is on your desk. It needs to be signed. → Upgrade: The document that needs to be signed is on your desk.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Add commas if the clause is EXTRA information. Leave it blank if it is ESSENTIAL.
The sun ___ which is a star ___ provides us with heat.
The shoes ___ that I bought yesterday ___ are too small.
My mother ___ who is a retired teacher ___ lives in Brazil.
Guided: Combine the sentences using a relative clause.
Apple released a new phone. It is very expensive. → Apple released a new phone, ____________________.
I am looking for the keys. They were on the table. → I am looking for the keys ____________________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write a sentence introducing a famous person from your country to a foreigner. Use a non-defining relative clause (with commas) to add an extra fact about them.

B1.G.14. Linking and discourse markers (B1 set)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Organize your thoughts in presentations and emails.
Show the logical relationship between two separate sentences.
Upgrade your writing from "casual" to "professional."
2) Form (how to build it)
These markers usually start a new sentence and are followed by a comma.
Contrast: However, / On the other hand,
Result: Therefore, / As a result,
Addition: In addition, / Furthermore,
Example: For example, / For instance,
Structure: [Sentence 1]. [Marker], [Sentence 2].
Example: The project was delayed. Therefore, we missed the deadline.
3) Meaning (what it means)
These are "signposts." They tell the reader what direction your brain is moving. However tells them to prepare for a U-turn. In addition tells them to keep walking straight.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use these primarily in writing (emails, essays, reports) or formal speaking (presentations). In casual speech, we prefer simpler words like but, so, and also.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Informal vs. Formal
Casual: It was raining, so we stayed inside.
Professional: It was raining heavily. As a result, we remained indoors.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Punctuation mistakes, like joining two full sentences with just a comma and a marker (The price is high, however, the quality is good).
Fix: These markers are strong. They need a period (or a semicolon) before them, and a comma after them. (The price is high. However, the quality is good).
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, porém and portanto can easily sit in the middle of a sentence surrounded by commas. Enforce the strict English punctuation boundary: Period, Capital Letter, Marker, Comma.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Also, we need more time. → Upgrade: In addition, we require an extension.
Starter: But it is too expensive. → Upgrade: However, the cost is prohibitive.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Choose the correct marker.
The company lost money this year. (Therefore / However), they will not pay bonuses.
Our product is highly durable. (For example / In addition), it comes with a 5-year warranty.
We liked the hotel. (As a result / However), the location was very noisy.
Guided: Finish the second sentence logically.
Learning English takes time. However, ____________________.
Traffic in the city is terrible. Therefore, ____________________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write two sentences about a new technology (like AI or electric cars). In sentence one, state a benefit. In sentence two, use However, to state a drawback.

B1.G.15. Clause combining: because/so/although/while
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Show contrast and surprise within a single sentence.
Balance competing ideas in a negotiation or discussion.
Improve the rhythm and flow of your speaking.
2) Form (how to build it)
Contrast/Surprise: Although / Even though
Although + [Unexpected Fact], + [Main Result].
Although it was raining, we went to the beach.
We went to the beach although it was raining.
Simultaneous actions or Contrast: While
While + [Action/Idea 1], + [Action/Idea 2].
While I agree with you on the price, I don't like the timeline.
3) Meaning (what it means)
Although sets up a hurdle or an obstacle, but shows that the main action happened anyway.
While can mean "at the same time" (time), but in B1, it is frequently used to mean "whereas" (contrast).
4) Use (when to choose it)
Although is much smoother and more advanced than just using but in the middle of a sentence. It allows you to put the negative information first and end on a positive note.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Although vs. However
Although joins two ideas in one sentence. (Although it was cold, I went swimming).
However connects two separate sentences. (It was cold. However, I went swimming).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using "Although" and "But" in the same sentence (Although it was raining, but we went out).
Fix: Choose one! If you open the door with Although, you don't need but to connect the rooms. The comma does the work.
L1 Transfer Note: Brazilian learners sometimes struggle with embora (although), preferring to default to mas (but). Force practice where they must start the sentence with Although.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: It is expensive but I will buy it. → Upgrade: Even though it's expensive, I'm going to buy it.
Starter: You want X, but I want Y. → Upgrade: While I understand your position, I have a different perspective.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Find and correct the mistake.
Although I was tired, but I couldn't sleep.
While I like the design. I hate the color.
However it is late, we are still working.
Guided: Combine the two ideas using Although.
He has a lot of experience. He didn't get the job. → ____________________.
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: Think of a movie or book you like that has a flaw. Tell a partner about it using Even though (e.g., "Even though the ending was bad, I still loved the characters").

B1.G.16. Indirect questions (core)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Be polite and respectful when talking to strangers or clients.
Ask sensitive or personal questions without sounding aggressive.
Ask for directions or help in a professional manner.
2) Form (how to build it)
Introductory Phrase + Question Word + Subject + Verb
Introductory Phrases:
Could you tell me...?
Do you know...?
I was wondering...
The Word Order Rule:
Once you use an introductory phrase, the second part of the sentence reverts to normal statement order (Subject + Verb). Do NOT use do, does, or did.
Direct Question (Aggressive)
Indirect Question (Polite)
Where is the bank?
Could you tell me where the bank is?
What time does it start?
Do you know what time it starts?
Why did he leave?
I was wondering why he left.

Yes/No Questions: Use if.
Are we late? → Do you know if we are late?
3) Meaning (what it means)
By adding a "softener" to the beginning of the sentence, you remove the demand. You are asking if the person has the knowledge, rather than demanding the information itself.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Mandatory when stopping someone on the street (e.g., asking for directions).
Highly recommended in professional emails, especially when asking for updates or favors.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Boss: "Where is the report?" (Direct - appropriate downward communication).
Employee: "Could you tell me where the report is?" (Indirect - appropriate upward communication).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Keeping the question word order (Could you tell me where is the bank? / Do you know what time does the train leave?).
Fix: The first part of the sentence (Could you tell me) is the question. The second part is just a fact. Drop the do/does/did and put the subject before the verb.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the word order doesn't change when you make a question indirect (Onde é o banco? -> Você sabe onde é o banco?). This is one of the biggest syntax hurdles for Brazilians at the B1 level. They must mentally process the swap.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Where is the bathroom? → Upgrade: Excuse me, do you happen to know where the restroom is?
Starter: Why did the client cancel? → Upgrade: Do we have any idea why the client canceled?
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Change the direct question into an indirect question.
Where does she live? → Do you know where ____________________?
How much does this cost? → Could you tell me how much ____________________?
Did the email arrive? → Do you know if ____________________?
Guided: Form a polite indirect question for these situations.
You are at an airport and need to find Gate 4. → Excuse me, could you tell me ____________________?
You want to ask your boss when the meeting is finishing. → I was wondering when ____________________?
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: Role-play stopping a stranger on the street in London. Politely ask them for directions to the nearest subway station, and ask if they know how much a ticket costs.

B1.G.17. Question tags & short responses (interaction)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Keep a conversation flowing naturally and include the listener.
Check if information you have is correct.
Soften a statement so it doesn't sound like a strict order.
2) Form (how to build it)
The Golden Rule: Opposites Attract
Positive Statement → Negative Tag
Negative Statement → Positive Tag
The Auxiliary Rule: Match the Verb
Verb 'Be': You are Brazilian, aren't you? / She isn't here, is she?
Simple Present/Past: You live in Blumenau, don't you? / He went home, didn't he?
Continuous/Perfect: They are working, aren't they? / You haven't finished, have you?
Modals: We can park here, can't we? / You won't tell anyone, will you?
3) Meaning (what it means)
Question tags turn a statement into a check for agreement.
Voice goes UP: You are genuinely asking a question because you aren't sure. (You sent the email, didn't you? ↗)
Voice goes DOWN: You are just making conversation and expect them to agree. (It's a beautiful day, isn't it? ↘)
4) Use (when to choose it)
Very common in British English, but used globally in spoken interactions. It is the verbal equivalent of nodding your head and asking the other person to nod with you.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
"You are from Brazil, right?" (Common, slightly informal).
"You are from Brazil, aren't you?" (Natural, engaging grammar).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using "no?" or "isn't it?" for everything (You like coffee, isn't it? / You went to the party, no?).
Fix: English does not have a universal tag. You must look at the verb in the first half of the sentence and reflect it in the tag.
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese has the ultimate universal tag: ...né? (não é?). This makes English question tags feel incredibly exhausting for Brazilians because they have to do mental math for every sentence. Emphasize that mastering tags is a massive leap toward sounding fluent.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Is this your jacket? → Upgrade: This is your jacket, isn't it?
Starter: Do you agree? → Upgrade: Makes sense, doesn't it?
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Add the correct question tag.
You don't like spicy food, _______________?
The meeting starts at 2:00, _______________?
They have already left, _______________?
Guided: Write a statement and a tag to check these facts.
You think your friend is tired. → You are ____________________?
You think your coworker didn't send the report. → You didn't ____________________?
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: Look out the window with a partner. Make three observations using question tags with falling intonation (e.g., "The weather is terrible today, isn't it?").

B1.G.18. Comparatives for persuasion
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Pitch a product by highlighting exactly how much better it is.
Analyze data and explain trends in a presentation.
Negotiate by showing the exact difference between two options.
2) Form (how to build it)
You already know comparatives (A1.G.15). Now, we add modifiers before the comparative to show the size of the difference.
Big Differences (A lot)
much / far / a lot / significantly + comparative
This software is much faster than the old one.
It is far more expensive to live in London.
Small Differences (A little)
a bit / slightly / a little + comparative
Today is a bit colder than yesterday.
Option B is slightly more efficient.
3) Meaning (what it means)
These modifiers act like a magnifying glass. They don't just say A > B; they tell the listener exactly how big the gap is between A and B.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Far and significantly are excellent for professional, formal data talk (e.g., "Sales are significantly higher this quarter").
A bit and a lot are perfect for everyday conversation.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
A1: This car is better.
B1: This car is far better. (Adding persuasive weight to your argument).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using "very" with comparatives (This is very better / It is very more expensive).
Fix: Very is for base adjectives (very good, very expensive). For comparatives, you must use much or a lot. (Much better, much more expensive).
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, muito translates to both very and much/a lot. Therefore, muito melhor often gets incorrectly translated as very better. Drill "much better" as a set phrase.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: It is more difficult. → Upgrade: It's a whole lot more difficult.
Starter: Our price is lower. → Upgrade: Our pricing is significantly more competitive.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check)
Controlled: Find and correct the mistake.
Traveling by train is very more relaxing than driving.
She is slightly taller that her sister.
This is far cheaper.
Guided: Look at the data and write a sentence.
Laptop A: $500. Laptop B: $2000. (expensive) → Laptop B is ____________________.
Flight time to Paris: 10 hours. Flight time to Rome: 10.5 hours. (long) → The flight to Rome is ____________________.

B1.G.19. Determiners: all/most/both/each/every
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Generalize about groups of people or sets of data.
Specify exactly how much of a group is involved in an action.
Write clear instructions for multiple items.
2) Form (how to build it)
Plural Nouns
All (100%): All employees need to attend.
Most (Majority): Most people like music.
Both (2 out of 2): Both options are good.
Singular Nouns
Every (All, but looking at them one by one): Every employee needs to attend.
Each (Individual focus): Each option has a different price.
3) Meaning (what it means) These words define the "scope" of your statement. Are you talking about the whole group as one big mass (all), or are you pointing to every single individual inside that group (every/each)?
4) Use (when to choose it)
All vs. Every: They mean the same thing, but the grammar changes. All takes a plural noun and a plural verb. Every takes a singular noun and a singular verb.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
All the people are here. (Focus on the crowd).
Every person is here. (Focus on the individuals making up the crowd).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using plural nouns and verbs after every or each (Every students are here / Each computers have a virus).
Fix: Every and each are strictly singular. Treat them like he/she/it. (Every student is here).
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, todos/todas covers "all" and "every", leading to a strong habit of using plurals. Brazilians often say "All the people is here" (treating people as singular like a galera/o povo) or "Every people are here." Drill the distinct singular/plural lanes.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: All the people in the office agreed. → Upgrade: Everyone in the office was on board.
Starter: Most of the time it works. → Upgrade: Nine times out of ten, it works perfectly.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose the correct determiner.
(All / Every) computers in the office need to be updated.
I have two brothers, and (both / all) of them live abroad.
(Each / Most) person will receive a unique password.
Guided: Finish the sentence with a logical deduction.
All the flights to New York were canceled today, so ____________________.
Every time I try to open this file, ____________________.

B1.G.20. Pronoun reference for cohesion
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Write smooth, connected paragraphs without repeating yourself.
Refer back to an entire idea or situation efficiently.
Sound like a fluent, organized speaker during presentations.
2) Form (how to build it)
Using It / They / This / That / Which to point backwards.
Pointing to a single noun: The company is expanding. It plans to hire 50 people.
Pointing to a plural noun: The clients are happy. They renewed the contract.
Pointing to a WHOLE IDEA: We lost the main client. This is a huge problem.
Pointing to a WHOLE IDEA (with a relative clause): We lost the main client, which is a huge problem.
3) Meaning (what it means) These pronouns act like anchors. They tie your current sentence to the sentence you just finished, pulling the reader smoothly along your train of thought.
4) Use (when to choose it)
This / That: Use these at the beginning of a new sentence to refer to a complex situation you just described. This feels closer and more immediate. That feels a bit more distant or finished.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Repetitive: We are upgrading the software. Upgrading the software will take two days. Upgrading the software will cost $500.
Cohesive: We are upgrading the software. This process will take two days, which will cost $500.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using "what" to refer back to a whole clause (He was late, what made the boss angry).
Fix: When pointing back to an entire situation separated by a comma, you must use which.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, o que is used perfectly in this context (Ele se atrasou, o que irritou o chefe). The direct translation to what is a classic B1 error.
Error: Dropping the pronoun completely (Is a huge problem).
Fix: English sentences need subjects. If there isn't a noun, you must use It or This.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I got a promotion. It is great. → Upgrade: I got a promotion, which I’m thrilled about.
Starter: The wifi is down. This is bad. → Upgrade: The wifi is down. This means we can't send the emails.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose which or what.
They offered me the job, ___________ was a huge surprise.
I don't know ___________ he wants.
The restaurant was closed, ___________ meant we had to eat at home.
Guided: Connect the sentences using This or which.
Our budget was cut by 20%. (This / mean) → ____________________.
He finally replied to my email. (which / be / a relief) → ____________________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write two sentences about a recent change in your life or workplace. In the second sentence, start with "This change..." or use ", which..." to comment on how you feel about it.

B1.G.21. Articles III
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Make broad, philosophical statements about life, society, and business.
Talk about daily routines involving institutions (work, school, hospital).
Polish your writing to remove distracting, unnatural errors.
2) Form (how to build it)
Rule 1: General vs. Specific (Plurals & Noncount nouns)
Zero Article (Ø): Talking about things in general.
Ø Computers are expensive. (All computers in the world).
Ø Love is complicated. (The concept of love).
The: Talking about specific things both the speaker and listener know.
The computers in this office are slow. (Specific ones).
Rule 2: Institutional Nouns (Places of purpose)
When you go to a place for its primary purpose, use the Zero Article (Ø).
go to Ø work (to do your job)
go to Ø bed (to sleep)
go to Ø school (to study)
go to Ø prison (as a prisoner)
3) Meaning (what it means) Articles tell the listener if you are pulling a specific file out of a cabinet ("the"), talking about a random file ("a/an"), or talking about the concept of files existing in the universe ("zero").
4) Use (when to choose it)
Only use the when you can mentally point at the object and say, "You know exactly which one I mean."
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
General vs. Specific
I love Ø dogs. (All dogs).
I love the dogs next door. (Those two specific animals).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting "the" in front of general plural nouns or abstract concepts (The life is hard / The Brazilians love soccer).
Fix: Drop the article. (Life is hard / Brazilians love soccer).
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese demands definite articles for general statements (A vida é dura / Os brasileiros amam futebol). This makes English sound completely wrong to Brazilian ears. This rule requires constant, conscious correction until it feels natural.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: The time is money. → Upgrade: As they say, time is money.
Starter: I go to the work at 8. → Upgrade: I head into work around 8.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Cross out "The" if it is incorrect.
The money makes the world go round.
Where is the money I left on the table?
The education is very important for the children.
Guided: Fill in the blanks with the or leave it blank (Ø).
I think ___________ technology is changing too fast.
Can you turn off ___________ TV? I'm trying to read.
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: State three general facts or opinions about your industry or hobby without using "the" (e.g., "Software engineering requires patience").

B1.G.22. Preposition patterns (expanded)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Use verbs correctly without having to pause and translate.
Express agreement, dependence, and focus in professional settings.
Sound highly accurate and idiomatic.
2) Form (how to build it)
Verbs and adjectives in English often lock onto a specific preposition. These are called "dependent prepositions." You must memorize the pair as a single unit.
High-Frequency B1 Pairs:
depend ON (not of)
rely ON
agree WITH a person / agree ON a topic
focus ON (not in)
pay FOR an item
wait FOR a person/thing
good AT a skill (not in)
responsible FOR a task
Rule: If a verb comes immediately after these prepositions, it MUST be in the -ing form.
I am responsible for managing the team.
She is good at solving problems.
3) Meaning (what it means) These prepositions don't always carry their normal spatial meaning (like "on the table"). They act as grammatical glue that connects the verb to its object.
4) Use (when to choose it)
These pairs are completely arbitrary. They evolved through history, not logic. The only way to choose the right one is to memorize the combination.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Pay the bill (Direct object - no preposition)
Pay FOR the coffee (Item - needs 'for')
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Direct translation of the preposition from the native language.
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese speakers constantly say depend of (depende de), focus in (focar em), and good in (bom em). You must rewire these connections. Create a mental link between the English verb and the English preposition from day one.
Error: Using the infinitive after a preposition (I am responsible for to lead).
Fix: Prepositions are allergic to the base verb. Always use the -ing noun form.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: It depends of the price. → Upgrade: That really depends on the price.
Starter: I agree with you. → Upgrade: I couldn't agree with you more.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose the correct preposition.
We need to focus (in / on / at) the new marketing strategy.
I am waiting (to / for / by) the manager to approve this.
Are you interested (in / on / about) joining the conversation club?
Guided: Finish the sentences truly using an -ing verb.
In my job, I am responsible for ____________________.
I am not very good at ____________________.
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: Role-play a discussion about a project timeline. Use depend on, focus on, and agree with to negotiate the details.

B1.G.23. Phrasal verbs (systematic)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Understand native speakers when they use informal, everyday vocabulary.
Sound less robotic and more conversational.
Explain physical actions and technical processes.
2) Form (how to build it)
A phrasal verb is a Verb + Particle (Preposition/Adverb) that creates a new meaning. (e.g., give = transfer possession; give up = quit).
Type 1: Inseparable (Cannot be split) The object must go after the particle.
I am looking for my keys.
I am looking for them. (NOT: looking them for).
Type 2: Separable (Can be split) The object can go in the middle OR at the end.
Please turn on the TV.
Please turn the TV on.
CRITICAL RULE: If the object is a pronoun (it, them, him, her), it MUST go in the middle.
Please turn it on. (NOT: turn on it).
3) Meaning (what it means) Sometimes the meaning is literal (stand up, sit down). Often, it is idiomatic (figure out = solve, put off = delay).
4) Use (when to choose it)
Phrasal verbs are the lifeblood of spoken English. While formal writing might prefer "postpone," a native speaker in a meeting will almost always say "put off."
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Formal/Written: Please extinguish the fire.
Natural/Spoken: Please put out the fire.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting the pronoun at the end of a separable phrasal verb (I picked up it / He threw away them).
Fix: Pronouns are light; they float to the middle. Nouns are heavy; they can sink to the end. (I picked it up / He threw them away).
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese does not have phrasal verbs. Translating them literally often creates nonsense. Treat them as entirely new vocabulary words, not as a verb + a direction.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Can you resolve the problem? → Upgrade: Can you figure this out?
Starter: I will search for the information. → Upgrade: I'll look into it.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Rewrite the sentence replacing the noun with it or them.
Please turn off the lights. → Please ____________________.
I need to throw away this garbage. → I need to ____________________.
She is looking after the children. → She is ____________________. (Hint: Inseparable!)
Guided: Replace the formal verb in parentheses with a common phrasal verb (e.g., figure out, look for, wake up).
I need to (solve) ___________ this math problem.
What time do you usually (awaken) ___________ on weekends?
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write instructions for how to use a coffee machine or a printer. Use at least three phrasal verbs (e.g., plug in, turn on, take out).

B1.G.24. Adjective order (basic)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Describe lost items to the police or at a front desk.
Write detailed product descriptions for sales or catalogs.
Paint a vivid picture when telling a story.
2) Form (how to build it)
When you use more than one adjective before a noun, English follows a strict, unwritten sequence.
The Basic OSACM Formula:
Opinion (beautiful, ugly, expensive, terrible)
Size (big, small, tall, long)
Age (old, new, antique, young)
Color (red, black, dark, light)
Material (wooden, plastic, metal, cotton)
NOUN
Examples:
A beautiful (Opinion) old (Age) wooden (Material) table.
A big (Size) black (Color) dog.
3) Meaning (what it means) The rule of thumb is: Subjective adjectives (opinions) go furthest away from the noun. Objective adjectives (facts you can't argue with, like material) sit closest to the noun.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Pragmatic note: Native speakers almost never use more than two or three adjectives at a time. It sounds unnatural. Limit yourself to a maximum of three before the noun.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Wrong: A red big car. (Native speakers will instinctively feel this sounds "weird" without knowing why).
Right: A big red car.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting the adjectives after the noun (A car big and red).
Fix: English is a front-loaded language. Adjectives go before the noun.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the noun comes first, followed by the descriptors (Um carro grande e vermelho). Reversing this order and then stacking the adjectives in a specific sequence is mentally taxing. Start by practicing just two adjectives at a time (Opinion + Color).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I bought a table. It is wooden and nice. → Upgrade: I bought a lovely wooden table.
Starter: I lost my jacket. It is leather and black. → Upgrade: I'm looking for a black leather jacket.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Put the adjectives in the correct order.
a / leather / black / nice / jacket → ____________________
some / Italian / delicious / hot / pizza → ____________________ (Hint: Opinion always first)
an / plastic / ugly / old / chair → ____________________
Guided: Describe these items using two adjectives in the correct order.
Your favorite shoes: ____________________
The phone you are using right now: ____________________

B1.G.25. “So/such” for emphasis
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Express strong emotions (frustration, excitement, gratitude).
Tell entertaining stories with dramatic flair.
Give enthusiastic compliments.
2) Form (how to build it)
SO + Adjective / Adverb
The movie was so good!
She drives so fast.
(Note: No noun follows the adjective).
SUCH + (a/an) + Adjective + Noun
It was such a good movie!
They are such nice people.
(Note: You must use a/an if the noun is singular and countable).
3) Meaning (what it means) Both words mean "very" or "really," but they add an emotional, dramatic emphasis. You are often implying "...that I couldn't believe it."
4) Use (when to choose it)
These are highly conversational and expressive.
They are often paired with a "that" clause to show a result: It was so cold that the lake froze.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Informative: The weather is very hot.
Expressive: The weather is so hot!
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Using so before an adjective + noun combination (It is a so beautiful day / She is a so smart woman).
Fix: If there is a noun at the end of the phrase, you must switch to such. (It is such a beautiful day).
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, tão is used for both structures (tão bonito and um dia tão bonito). Because so translates to tão, Brazilians frequently say "a so beautiful day." Drill the formula: "So + Adjective STOP. Such a + Adjective + Noun."
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Thank you very much. → Upgrade: Thank you so much. / You've been such a big help.
Starter: It is a very big problem. → Upgrade: It's such a headache.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose so, such, or such a.
I am ___________ tired.
That was ___________ good idea!
They gave us ___________ terrible advice. (Hint: advice is noncount)
Guided: Rewrite the sentence using the word in parentheses.
The exam was very difficult. (so) → ____________________.
He is a very talented musician. (such) → ____________________.

B1.G.26. “Too/enough” (productive)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Explain constraints in a project (lack of time, budget, or resources).
Justify why a proposed solution won't work.
Set parameters for what you need to complete a task.
2) Form (how to build it)
You learned the basics in A1. Now, we add nouns and infinitive phrases (to do) to explain what the problem stops us from doing.
Too + Adjective + (for someone) + (to do something)
The software is too expensive to buy.
This report is too complex for me to finish today.
Enough + Noun + (to do something)
We don't have enough money to hire a new designer.
Do we have enough chairs for everyone?
Adjective + Enough + (to do something)
The wifi isn't fast enough to stream the video.
3) Meaning (what it means) This grammar creates a logical block. "Too" means the barrier is too high to cross. "Not enough" means you don't have the fuel to reach the goal.
4) Use (when to choose it)
This is the language of troubleshooting and budget meetings. It is the most natural way to push back on unrealistic requests without just saying a blunt "No."
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Basic: We don't have money, so we can't buy it.
Professional: We don't have enough money to buy it.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Putting enough after the noun (We don't have money enough).
Fix: Enough comes after adjectives (big enough), but before nouns (enough money).
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, suficiente usually goes after the noun (dinheiro suficiente). This requires conscious rewiring for Brazilian learners. Drill the rhythm: Big enough / Enough money.
Error: Using too much before adjectives (The test was too much difficult).
Fix: Drop the much. Use too directly before adjectives (too difficult).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: The deadline is too short. → Upgrade: That deadline is a little too tight for us to meet.
Starter: I don't have enough time. → Upgrade: I simply don't have enough hours in the day to get this done.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Put the words in the correct order.
experience / enough / she / for / have / the job / doesn't.
too / is / this / drink / to / coffee / hot.
loud / enough / hear / the / wasn't / speaker / to.
Guided: Explain the problem using too or enough.
Why can't we launch the product today? (bugs / software) → There are ____________________.
Why didn't you finish the test? (time) → I didn't have ____________________.

B1.G.27. Basic noun clauses
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
State your professional opinion clearly and politely.
Express the importance or urgency of a task.
Report facts or beliefs without taking full ownership of them.
2) Form (how to build it)
A noun clause is an entire mini-sentence (Subject + Verb) that acts like a single noun (an object) inside a bigger sentence. They are usually connected by that.
Expressing Stance (Opinion/Belief)
I think (that) this is a good idea.
We believe (that) the market will grow.
Expressing Importance (It + Adjective)
It is important (that) we sign the contract today.
It is essential (that) everyone attends the meeting.
3) Meaning (what it means) Noun clauses act as a "cushion." Instead of stating a raw fact ("The market will grow"), you cushion it with your stance ("I believe that..."). It shows the listener how you relate to the information.
4) Use (when to choose it)
In spoken English, the word that is almost always dropped. (I think we should go).
In written English, keeping that makes the sentence feel more formal and structured.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Aggressive Fact: We must lower the price.
Professional Stance: I feel that we must lower the price. / It's crucial that we lower the price.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Translating subjunctive triggers (It is important that he goes vs. It is important that he go).
Fix: At the B1 level, do not worry about the subjunctive mood. Just use the normal present tense (It's important that he goes).
L1 Transfer Note: Portuguese requires the subjunctive here (É importante que ele vá). Reassure learners that English is much simpler; they can just plug in a regular present tense sentence after that.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I think it is too expensive. → Upgrade: My concern is that it might be too expensive.
Starter: We need to fix this fast. → Upgrade: It's critical that we get this resolved quickly.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Combine the two ideas using a that clause.
Idea: We are over budget. Stance: The manager noticed. → The manager noticed ____________________.
Idea: The client is unhappy. Stance: I am worried. → I am worried ____________________.
Guided: Finish the sentence to express your stance.

B1.G.28. “The more…, the more…” (intro)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Explain cause and effect relationships simply.
Show how two changing situations are linked together.
Make persuasive arguments in sales or negotiations.
2) Form (how to build it)
The + Comparative, the + Comparative
The more you practice**, the better** you get.
The faster we finish**, the sooner** we can go home.
The bigger the risk**, the higher** the reward.
Note: The comma in the middle is mandatory.
3) Meaning (what it means) This structure shows a direct correlation. If you increase the dial on the left side of the comma, the dial on the right side of the comma moves automatically.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Extremely common in business to explain pricing, risks, or timelines. (The more you buy, the cheaper it gets).
It is also a very natural way to give advice.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Wordy: If we leave earlier, we will have less traffic.
Punchy: The earlier we leave, the less traffic there will be.
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Forgetting the word "The" (More I study, more I learn).
Fix: The structure absolutely requires "The" at the beginning of both clauses. It acts as the anchor.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the exact same structure exists (Quanto mais... mais...). However, because there is no direct equivalent to "The" in the Portuguese phrase, Brazilian learners almost always drop it. Drill the rhythm: THE more... THE more...
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: If you study more, it gets easier. → Upgrade: The more you study, the easier it gets.
Starter: A short email is good. → Upgrade: When it comes to emails, the shorter, the better. (A common idiom).
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Match the first half to the logical second half.

When buying a laptop, the more memory it has, the ____________________.

B1.G.29. “Be supposed to” (expectations)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Talk about rules that people often break or ignore.
Explain what the original plan was when things go wrong.
Gently remind someone of their responsibilities.
2) Form (how to build it)
Subject + be (am/is/are/was/were) + supposed to + Base Verb
Present (General Rules/Expectations)
We are supposed to wear a tie. (But maybe we don't always do it).
I am not supposed to tell you this. (It's a secret, but I'm going to tell you anyway).
Past (Failed Plans)
The meeting was supposed to start at 9:00. (It is 9:15 and it hasn't started yet).
I was supposed to call him yesterday. (I forgot).
3) Meaning (what it means) Be supposed to deals with expectations. It implies there is a plan or a rule, but it also strongly hints that the reality is different from the plan.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use the present tense to explain "soft rules" or social etiquette.
Use the past tense to make excuses or explain why a schedule fell apart.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Have to vs. Supposed to
We have to wear a uniform. (Strict rule. If you don't, you are fired).
We are supposed to wear a uniform. (The rule exists, but the boss doesn't care if we wear jeans on Fridays).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Dropping the verb be or the 'd' in supposed (I supposed to go / He is suppose to call).
Fix: It is a three-part machine: Be + Supposed + To. You need all three pieces.
L1 Transfer Note: This fills a massive gap for Portuguese speakers. In Portuguese, you use era para (it was for) to talk about failed plans (Era para a reunião começar às 9). Be supposed to is the exact English equivalent of era para. Once learners make this connection, their fluency jumps significantly.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: The plan was to finish today, but we didn't. → Upgrade: We were supposed to finish today.
Starter: You can't park here. → Upgrade: You're not really supposed to park here. (Much softer and more polite).
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose the correct form.
I (am suppose to / am supposed to) send the report by 5 PM.
They (were supposed to / was supposed to) deliver the package yesterday.
You (aren't supposed to / don't supposed to) use your phone during the movie.
Guided: Explain the failed plan.
Why didn't you go to the gym yesterday? → I was supposed to go, but ____________________.
Why are you wearing a suit today? → We are supposed to ____________________ when clients visit.

B1.G.30. Participles as adjectives (bored/boring)
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)

Avoid giving the wrong impression about your personality.
2) Form (how to build it)
Many verbs of emotion (bore, interest, tire, confuse) can be turned into two different adjectives.
The -ED Adjective (The Receiver)
Describes how a person feels.
I am bored.
She is interested in the project.
We were confused by the email.
The -ING Adjective (The Cause)
Describes the thing/person that creates the feeling.
The meeting is boring.
The project is interesting.
The email was confusing.
3) Meaning (what it means)
-ED = Internal feeling (I received the emotion).
-ING = External source (It is pushing the emotion out into the world).
4) Use (when to choose it)
Only humans (and sometimes animals) can be -ed (bored, excited, surprised).
Things and situations are almost always -ing (boring, exciting, surprising).
Exception: A person can be boring (meaning they put other people to sleep!).
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
The disastrous mistake:
I am bored. (I have nothing to do. Please entertain me).
I am boring. (I am an uninteresting person with a terrible personality).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Mixing them up when talking about feelings (I am very boring today / I am very confusing).
Fix: Remember: ED = Emotion. ING = Thing.
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, the distinction is made with completely different words or reflexive verbs (Estou chateado vs É chato / Estou confuso vs É confuso). Because English uses the same root word (bore/confuse), Brazilians frequently say "I am boring" when they mean estou entediado. This requires heavy, humorous correction in class.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: The movie was boring. → Upgrade: I was completely bored watching that movie.
Starter: I am interested in this. → Upgrade: I find this really interesting.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Choose the correct adjective.
The instructions for this software are very (confused / confusing).
I was (surprised / surprising) to see him at the office on a Sunday.
My new job is incredibly (tired / tiring). I sleep for 10 hours every night.
Guided: Describe these things using an -ing adjective, and how they make you feel using an -ed adjective.
A long flight: The flight is ___________, so I usually feel ___________.
A good thriller movie: The movie is ___________, so I feel ___________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write a two-sentence review of the last book or article you read. Describe the content (using -ing) and your reaction to it (using -ed).

B1.G.31. Basic formal vs informal grammar choices
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Adjust your tone depending on who you are talking to (boss vs. friend).
Sound more relaxed and natural in casual conversations.
Maintain professional distance in written reports.
2) Form (how to build it)
Grammar changes based on "Register" (how formal the situation is).
Choice 1: Contractions
Informal (Speaking/Chats): I'm writing to tell you that we've finished.
Formal (Reports/Official Emails): I am writing to inform you that we have finished.
Choice 2: Ellipsis (Dropping words)
Informal: (Are you) Going to lunch? / (It) Sounds good. / (I will) See you later.
Formal: Are you going to lunch? / That sounds good. / I will see you later.
Choice 3: Phrasal Verbs vs. Latinate Verbs
Informal: We need to put off the meeting.
Formal: We need to postpone the meeting.
3) Meaning (what it means) Formal grammar is wearing a suit; it is complete, precise, and keeps a respectful distance. Informal grammar is wearing jeans; it is efficient, relaxed, and assumes you are close to the listener.
4) Use (when to choose it)
Use informal choices on Slack, WhatsApp, and when speaking to colleagues.
Use formal choices in cover letters, contracts, and emails to new, high-value clients.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Mixing registers (The "weird" sound): "Dear Sir, what's up? I am writing to inquire if you wanna grab coffee." (Do not mix a suit jacket with sweatpants).
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Writing academic or professional emails with contractions (The company hasn't achieved its goals).
Fix: When writing a formal essay or report, use the "Find" feature on your computer to search for apostrophes (') and expand them all to the full words (has not).
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: I do not know. → Upgrade: I don't know. (Speak with contractions!).
Starter: Do you want a coffee? → Upgrade: Want a coffee? (Drop the auxiliary in casual speech).
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Identify if the sentence is Formal (F) or Informal (I).
We have received your application. (___)
Got your email! (___)
Please let me know if you need anything. (___)
Guided: Rewrite this formal sentence into a casual Slack message to a coworker by using contractions and dropping unnecessary words.
"I am going to grab a coffee. Do you want anything?" → ____________________.
Mini-output: * Speaking prompt: Look at a formal request (e.g., "Would you mind assisting me with this file?"). Role-play asking the same question to your best friend using informal grammar.

B1.G.32. Grammar for email basics
1) What you can do with it (functional goal)
Write clear, polite, and actionable business emails.
Make requests without sounding bossy or demanding.
Bridge the gap between general English and professional communication.
2) Form (how to build it)
This chapter combines several B1 grammar points specifically for the medium of email.
Polite Requests (Modals + Indirect Questions)
Too direct: Send me the file.
Better: Could you send me the file?
Best: Would it be possible to send me the file?
Using "Just" as a Softener Adding "just" before verbs makes an email feel friendly rather than aggressive.
I am following up on my previous email. → I am just following up on my previous email.
I wanted to ask a question. → I just wanted to ask a question.
Action Framing (Present Continuous vs. Will)
I am attaching the document. (Focus on right now).
I will send the update tomorrow. (Focus on a promise/next step).
3) Meaning (what it means) In emails, you don't have body language or tone of voice to show you are friendly. You must use grammar (softeners, modals) to create that polite "smile."
4) Use (when to choose it)
These structures should be your default setting for workplace communication until you know a colleague very well.
5) Contrast (what it competes with)
Internal translation (often rude): I want that you do this by Friday.
Professional standard: Could you please have this done by Friday?
6) Common learner problems (and fixes)
Error: Starting emails with "I doubt..." to mean "I have a question."
L1 Transfer Note: In Portuguese, Eu tenho uma dúvida is the standard way to say you have a question. If translated literally ("I have a doubt"), it sounds like you do not believe the person. Always use: "I have a question" or "I just wanted to clarify..."
Error: Over-apologizing for bad English.
Fix: Instead of "Sorry for my bad English," use clear, short, bulleted sentences to ensure clarity. Grammar is a tool for clarity, not a test.
7) Task language upgrades (from basic → more natural)
Starter: Please read the attached file. → Upgrade: Please find attached the file for your review.
Starter: Tell me when you are free. → Upgrade: Let me know what time works best for you.
8) Micro-practice (short, self-check) Controlled: Soften these direct sentences by adding Could you or just.
Give me an update. → ____________________?
I am checking in on the project. → I am ____________________.
I need to change the meeting time. → I ____________________.
Guided: Rewrite this aggressive email to sound professional.
"Where is the report? I want it now." → ____________________.
Mini-output: * Writing prompt: Write a short, three-line email to a client. Use Just to state the purpose, use a polite request to ask for a document, and sign off professionally.
